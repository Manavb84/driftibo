01

The two ways to get inventory without tie-ups

You do not need to sign deals with hotels to sell rooms and packages. Two B2B channels already did that work for you. Understanding how each actually moves money and risk is the foundation for everything else.

Route A

Bedbanks & B2B portals

Wholesalers (TBO, Hotelbeds, RezLive…) buy hotel rooms in bulk at net rates and resell them to any registered travel business through a login or an API. You never contract the hotel.

  • How you join: register a business + GST/PAN, get approved in 24h–few days (some, like Hotelbeds, take 2–6 weeks).
  • Access: web portal for manual booking, or XML/JSON API to wire into your own site.
  • You pay by: prepaid wallet / deposit, or “book now, pay later” credit (a deposit or bank guarantee backs your credit line).
  • Voucher: issued instantly at confirmation, on the bedbank's paper (often white-label-able).
  • Who carries the risk: you do. Once confirmed, a no-show is treated as a late cancel — you can owe from one night up to 100% of the booking.
  • Margin: net rates run 15–40% below public price; you set a markup; effective gross ~12–28%.
Route B

DMCs & ground handlers

A Destination Management Company is a local specialist that has contracted the hotels, jeeps, guides and permits in its region and sells you the bundle at net rates via a B2B agreement.

  • How you join: a bilateral B2B agreement; show your credentials (IATO/ADTOI/IATA or just GST) + your volume in that region; approved in 1–2 days.
  • Access: portal login or quote-by-email/WhatsApp; the DMC builds the itinerary.
  • You pay by: 20–30% deposit to confirm, balance 45–60 days before travel (net-15/30 credit comes after you build a track record).
  • Voucher: issued at confirmation, usually white-labelled in your agency's name.
  • Who carries the risk: the DMC absorbs operational failure (overbooking, a no-show driver — it must fix it), but your deposit is non-refundable if the client cancels.
  • Margin: 8–18% on resort+transfer packages; 25–45% on bespoke / luxury itineraries.

Offbeat sourcing — your real supply chain

The places that look like abroad but aren't are exactly the ones bedbanks don't carry. Here you buy from regional DMCs, community cooperatives and state homestay registries — and you vet them like a pro, because your deposit and your reputation ride on them.

How to find & vet a regional DMC

Vetting checklist (run before you wire a rupee)

  • Check Ministry of Tourism recognition on etraveltradeapproval.nic.in
  • Confirm IATO / ADTOI listing (and ATOAI for trekking/adventure)
  • Validate the GST number; ask for a liability/indemnity COI naming you
  • Get 2+ agent references and a direct-to-property confirmation, not just the DMC's word
  • Red flags: Western Union/crypto/UPI-only requests, no track record, vague address

Contract terms to negotiate

  • Rate type: FIT (per-traveller net) vs group (10+ pax, typically 10–30% cheaper)
  • Allotment: hard (20–35% off but you pay even if unsold) · soft/free-sale with a 14–21 day release (10–25% off, lower risk) · on-request (5–15% off, zero risk)
  • Payment: aim 20–25% deposit + balance 7–14 days pre-arrival; offbeat/trek DMCs may want balance 60 days out
  • Margin reality: DMC packages give you ~8–18%; your own bundled experiences can reach 15–25%+

Getting the same seat cheaper — in plain words

There is a wholesale layer under the price you see on Google Flights. Three ways to reach it: a consolidator (buys seats in bulk at secret “net” fares and resells to you), group/series fares (a block of 10+ seats held for a fixed departure), and NDC (the airline's own modern feed, which carries bundle deals and fare types the old GDS screens miss). You don't need IATA for any of this — you book through a portal that holds the accreditation, and you keep the gap between the net price and what you charge the customer.

How a ticket actually gets issued “for you”

You register on a B2B portal (Riya, TBO, Agent Bazar…). The portal holds the IATA number and the BSP settlement account — the airline's money pipe. You search, pay from a prepaid wallet or credit line, and the portal issues the ticket in its own name. You collect the customer's fare and keep the spread. BSP settlement happens invisibly between the portal and the airline. At no point do you need your own IATA.

The package-bundling GST angle

Sell a flight on its own and your commission is taxed at 18% GST (air-agent service, SAC 998551). Fold the same flight into a tour package (flight + stay + transport, one bill) and the whole bundle sits on the 5% tour-operator slab (no ITC), effective from 22 Sep 2025. For the customer it's one clean all-in price; for you it's simpler invoicing — see §7 and §10.

Charter flights — with zero of your own capital

Helicopters and private jets sound like something only an airline can sell. They aren't. You sell them the same way you sell a hotel room you don't own — as a broker. You never buy an aircraft, never hold a licence, never carry the risk.

In plain words — the broker model

A helicopter or jet operator needs a DGCA NSOP (Non-Scheduled Operator Permit) — that's their burden, it regulates flying, not selling. A broker just connects a customer to an operator: you ask 2–3 operators for a quote, pick the best, add your markup, and quote the customer one price. The chain is Operator → Broker → you (Driftibo) → customer. All you need is your normal business + GST registration ✓ Verified (DGCA Aircraft Rules 1937 Rule 134A binds the aircraft operator, not the sales chain).

Who takes what

  • A pure broker typically earns 5–10% of the charter cost (real-world band runs wider, ~3–20% by deal). ✓ Verified
  • You, reselling as a tour operator, add your own markup on top of the broker/operator quote — 10–15% is normal for a leisure package.
  • GST: private charter is 18% (HSN 9964); the government pilgrimage shuttle is 5%. Fold charter into a package and it rides the 5% slab.
  • Deposit flow: operators want ~50% to confirm; collect 25–50% from your client first, balance 7–15 days out. Lead time 7–10 days (3 hr possible at a premium).

Empty legs — the cheap-seat hack

When an aircraft has to fly back empty after a one-way drop, the operator sells that leg cheap to recover fuel — ~25–75% off, typically 50–65% ⚑ Corrected (our earlier “50–75% floor” was the marketing best-case; the real floor is ~25%).

  • The window is brutal: ~78% of empty-leg bookings confirm within 7 days of departure, 41% within 48 hours.
  • Track via JetSetGo “JetSteals” app alerts, BookMyJet empty-legs, Club One Air's WhatsApp/newsletter.
  • Sell it to clients as a “flash departure” — only works if they're flexible on the exact date.

Same broker logic. India had 121 charter aircraft and 133 NSOP operators by mid-2025 (largest fleet in Asia-Pacific) — you need none of that to resell. Under 4 pax, business class + a premium cab is cheaper.

Local ground transport & transfers

Transport is 40–50% of an offbeat trip's cost and the lowest-margin line in travel — so where you source it decides whether a package makes money. In plain words: there are three ways to get a vehicle, and on hill routes only two of them actually work.

1 · B2B aggregator portals

Book transfers/cabs through a portal at net rates, set your own markup.

  • TBO Transfers — free signup, markup model, airport/city legs
  • Savaari via MMT myPartner — outstation cabs, 2,000+ cities, commission model
  • RezLive — hotel+transfer+sightseeing in one cart

In the hills, you must use the local union — and you tie up directly.

How to build & price a package — step by step

If you've never costed a trip: list every rupee you actually pay suppliers, mark it up to cover your profit, then add 5% GST on the total. That last number is the sticker price. Here it is worked all the way through.

The 8 cost components

  1. Stay — per room per night (÷2 for twin-share)
  2. Intercity transport — car/tempo + driver allowance (40–50% of cost)
  3. Local / sightseeing transport
  4. Activities & entry fees — guide day-charge, monuments
  5. Permits — ILP, forest fees (Chopta: ₹150/pax)
  6. Guide / trip leader — per day
  7. Meals — usually B+D, ~₹350/pax/meal
  8. Contingency — 5–8% for Himalayan routes

The formula (memorise this)

Sticker price = Net Cost ÷ (1 − margin%) × 1.05

At a 20% gross margin that's a quick Net Cost × 1.3125 (25% markup, then ×1.05 for GST).

Markup ≠ margin: 25% markup on cost = ~20% margin on price. Conversion: margin = markup ÷ (1+markup). So 33% markup = 25% margin; 43% markup = 30% margin.

Targets (offbeat India): group fixed-departures 15–20% margin · FIT/surprise 22–30% · luxury curated 35–40%. Net profit after your own time/marketing ≈ 8–15%.

FIT vs group — the transport split

The cost driver is intercity transport, because it's fixed and gets divided by heads:

  • Group (10 pax, Tempo ₹25,000): transport = ₹2,500/pax → net ~₹9,500 → sell ~₹12,500/pax
  • FIT (2 pax, Innova ₹16,000): transport = ₹8,000/pax → net ~₹15,850 → sell ~₹20,500/pax
  • FIT runs ~60% higher/head. Most early WhatsApp bookings are FIT (couples, 2–3 friends) — don't quote group rates to small groups.
  • Group needs a fill floor (≈8 of 10 seats) or margin collapses.

The surprise-trip pricing twist

You can't name a price for a hidden place — so price by tier band:

  • Cost out the most expensive destination in the tier; that's your baseline.
  • Apply markup, add 5% GST. If the revealed place is cheaper, the gap is pure buffer (5–8%) — never a loss.
  • Example bands: A “Nearest Escape” (Chopta/Gokarna) ₹14–18k · B “Mid-Distance” (Spiti/Ziro) ₹26–34k · C “Far & Wild” (Ladakh/Meghalaya) ₹40–55k (pre-GST).
  • Invoice still describes the service (“4N twin, transport, B+D, 1 guide-day, permits — destination disclosed 7 days prior”). T&Cs must state no refund after reveal.

  1. Day 0Enquiry — reply within 2 hrs; capture dates, pax, budget band, dietary/medical via 5 WhatsApp questions or a Google Form.

  3. Day 1–2Quote — show price + GST as two lines; state advance + balance; hold the price 48 hrs.
  4. Day 2–3Confirm + advance — collect 30–50%; send booking reference + GST invoice (state “inclusive of accommodation & transport”).

Tax & compliance

⚑ Corrected — important  GST on packages is not a “5% or 18%” choice

For a tour operator selling a bundled package, 5% GST without input tax credit (ITC) is mandatory — it is not a free choice against an 18% alternative (CBIC Notif. 11/2017 Entry 23(iii); confirmed by the Delhi AAR TUI India ruling that the 18%-with-ITC option “is not available”). The 18% with ITC figure belongs to a different classification: a travel agent/intermediary earning commission, who pays 18% only on the commission, not on gross package value. So your structure decides your rate — it isn't a slab you pick.

Worked example — why structure matters

A ₹1,00,000 Spiti package, your cost ₹85,000, margin ₹15,000:

  • As tour operator (principal): 5% GST on the gross ₹1,00,000 = ₹5,000, collected from the customer; you cannot reclaim the GST baked into hotels/transport. Sticker price ₹1,05,000.
  • As pure commission agent: 18% GST on your ₹15,000 fee = ₹2,700; the supplier invoices the customer directly. Lighter GST, but you're a facilitator, not the principal.

Domestic packages also went to 5% (no ITC) from 22 Sep 2025. Hotels ₹1,001–7,500/night = 5%; above ₹7,500 = 18%.

Money mechanics

The core cash-flow problem of this business: you pay suppliers before travel and get paid commission after — so you bridge it with customer advances and a reserve. Design your collection schedule to match your supplier deadlines.

The timing mismatch (and how to bridge it)

  • Suppliers want: 30–50% deposit within 48h, balance 45–60 days before travel.
  • Hotel commission arrives: 30–45 days after checkout.
  • So collect from customers: 25–30% at booking → 50% at 60 days out → balance at 30 days; 100% upfront if travel is within 14 days.
  • Keep a reserve: 2–3 months of operating expenses, separate from client funds.

Margins to plan around

  • Hotels on commission: 10–15%; transfers 15–20%; tours/activities 15–25%.
  • Net-rate markup (mid-range): 20–30%.
  • Target gross 18–25% → net profit 8–15% after overheads.
  • Bespoke offbeat bundles (your edge): 15–25%+.

How travel agents actually make money

“Commission” is the smallest part of how a good agency earns. The real money is in buying wholesale and selling retail, then stacking small extras on top. Here's where every rupee of margin actually comes from — and the honest, legal way to capture it.

The one idea that changes everything: net-rate arbitrage

Earning 10–12% commission on a ₹40,000 booking pays you ₹4,000–4,800. Offbeat destinations (Chopta, Spiti, Gokarna) have no public price to compare against, so the markup holds. This single lever is what funds an organic content business with no outside capital.

Risk & trust

Selling travel insurance — the legal route ✓ Verified

You can't just resell policies — you need IRDAI authorisation. Three legal routes, in rising order of cost/capability:

  • POSP (Point of Sales Person): a person (you or staff) does 15 hrs training + exam and is appointed under a licensed sponsor — note POSPs aren't directly “IRDAI-registered”; the sponsor holds the licence. Tied to one insurer. Travel insurance is an approved POSP product (sum insured up to ₹3 cr).
  • Corporate Agent: the agency itself registers with IRDAI (~₹50L net worth, a Principal Officer with 50-hr training) and can tie up with up to 9 general insurers. IRDAI's own portal confirms “a travel agent may offer travel insurance as a corporate agent.”
  • Broker: full registration, broadest product access (higher capital + PI cover).

Commission: no fixed caps since Apr 2024 (set within the insurer's EOM limit). 2026: registrations are now perpetual (annual fee + compliance) rather than fixed 3-year terms.

Carrying your own liability

  • Professional Indemnity (PI) — covers errors, negligent advice, defence costs (premium ~0.5–1.5% of cover; claims-made, so buy tail cover when winding a season down).
  • Commercial General Liability (CGL) — bodily injury on transfers, boat/animal rides, adventure activities.
  • Indian indemnity caps reach ₹35 crore; PI+CGL together is the standard tour-operator structure.

Supplier & payment fraud — the patterns to refuse

What scammers do

  • Fake DMCs/wholesalers take your deposit for inventory they don't control.
  • Cloned hotel/OTA sites (e.g. “booking-deals.com”) collect for non-existent rooms — peaks around Char Dham/Haj & long weekends.
  • Customer chargeback abuse: book on card, travel, then dispute as “unauthorised”.
  • Phishing/BEC: fake IATA/BSP invoices to a spoofed account.

Your defences

  • Verify suppliers on IATAN CheckACode; insist on direct-to-property confirmation codes.
  • Never pay suppliers by Western Union/crypto; call a known number to confirm wire details.
  • Keep signed booking agreements, vouchers, PNRs — your evidence in a chargeback (you have a 90-day window to submit).
  • Take big payments by card/bank transfer (reversible) over UPI.

Trust & the law you must publish

  • Terms & Conditions must state, in plain language: price breakdown, cancellation tiers + deadlines, refund timelines, force majeure, and a dispute/grievance path.
  • Grievance Officer (IT Rules 2021): publish contact; acknowledge in 24h, resolve in 15 days, if you run a site/app.
  • Don't over-charge cancellation: the CCPA is (May 2026) probing OTAs charging cancellation fees far above the airline's actual fee — fees above the supplier's real levy are an “unfair trade practice”.
  • Refunds go back to the original instrument; RBI's Payment Aggregator rules + CPA 2019 both bite if you stall.
