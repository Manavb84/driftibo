Registrations & legal (India)

In plain words — what "registering" actually means for you

Starting a travel business in India is simpler than most founders fear. Think of it in three layers. First, the business entity — the legal shell that owns your bank account, signs contracts with hotels and suppliers, and stands behind your invoices. Second, the tax number — your GST (Goods and Services Tax) registration, which is mandatory once you cross a revenue threshold. Third, optional credentials — government recognition stamps and trade-body memberships that signal you're serious, but are not legally required on Day 1. The popular belief that travel agencies in India need absolutely no licence is almost true — but read the correction below before assuming it applies to your specific situation.

The hard requirements are small: a registered entity + GST. Most "credentials" are voluntary credibility. But the popular line that there's no licence at all is not quite true — read the correction.

⚑ Corrected  "India needs no travel-agent licence" — mostly true, with real exceptions
  • ✅ For a general tour operator (packages, not ticketing): correct — just incorporate + GST. MoT (Ministry of Tourism) recognition is voluntary.
  • ⚠️ If you sell airline tickets directly, that's a separate mandatory DGCA (Directorate General of Civil Aviation) licence (Air Travel Agent), needing ~₹1 crore net worth + office. Selling flights via a B2B (Business-to-Business) portal avoids this — the portal holds the licence.
  • ⚠️ Several states mandate their own registration independent of the centre: Delhi (₹5,000 + ₹10,000 refundable deposit, 5 yrs), Assam, and Haryana (Registration & Regulation of Travel Agents Act 2025, in force April 2026). Check your state of operation.

Entity + the only mandatory step

How "picking a business structure" actually works — a 2-minute walk-through

When you start a business in India, you choose a legal shell. A Proprietorship is the simplest: you and the business are legally the same person, so setup is almost free — but if the business owes money, creditors can come after your personal savings too. An LLP (Limited Liability Partnership) costs ~₹5–15K to register and creates a wall between business debts and your personal finances. A Pvt Ltd (Private Limited Company) costs ~₹20–30K to incorporate, carries the most credibility with investors and large suppliers, and is taxed at roughly 25% of profits — but comes with more paperwork. For most early-stage travel founders aiming to raise money later, an LLP or Pvt Ltd is the right call.

  • Structure: Proprietorship (simplest, taxed at your personal income slab) · LLP (limited liability, ~₹5–15K to set up) · Pvt Ltd (best for raising money, ~₹20–30K, ~25% effective tax).
  • GST registration — mandatory once turnover crosses ₹20 lakh (₹10L in special-category/NE/hill states) ✓ Verified. The ₹40L threshold is goods-only and does not apply to you. Inter-state supply or acting as agent for another taxable person → register regardless of turnover (CGST §24).
  • Free Day-1 wins: Udyam (MSME — Micro, Small and Medium Enterprise) registration — unlocks collateral-free MUDRA loans; Shop & Establishment registration once you take commercial premises.

Ministry of Tourism recognition — voluntary, a Year-2 milestone

In plain words — what MoT recognition is and why you cannot get it yet

The Ministry of Tourism offers an official "recognised" stamp in five categories (Travel Agent, Inbound Tour Operator, Domestic Tour Operator, Adventure Tour Operator, Tourist Transport). This is not a licence — you can legally operate without it. What it buys you is supplier trust: hotels, foreign embassies issuing group-visa letters, and overseas wholesale buyers all treat a Ministry-recognised operator more seriously than an unknown entity. The catch: you cannot even apply until you have been trading for at least one full year and have four paid staff — so this is firmly a Year-2 goal, not a Day-1 requirement.

Tax & compliance

Decision you must make before raising your first invoice: principal or agent?

Your GST rate is not a slab you choose — it is locked in by the role you play in the transaction. Answer this one question first:

You are the PRINCIPAL if you sell the trip as your own product: you bundle the hotels, transport, and guide into a single package, you invoice the customer in your own name, and you are legally responsible if something goes wrong.
→ 5% GST on the entire package price. Mandatory — you cannot opt out of this rate.
You are the AGENT if you only arrange the trip on behalf of a supplier: the supplier invoices the customer directly, and you earn a commission or service fee for facilitating.
→ 18% GST on your commission/fee only — never on the full trip price.

The 18%-on-commission rate is not an alternative for the same business — it belongs to a different business model. If you sell bundled packages in your own name, you are the principal and 5% applies.

In plain words — the two taxes that will touch every booking you make

There are two taxes you will deal with on almost every sale. The first is GST (Goods and Services Tax). Think of it as a sales tax: you add a percentage on top of your price, the customer pays it, and you pass it to the government minus any GST already paid up the chain. The second is TCS (Tax Collected at Source), which applies specifically to overseas travel packages: you collect a small extra percentage from the customer and deposit it with the government as a prepayment of the customer's own income tax. Both add to the sticker price your customer sees. Getting either one wrong creates penalties that can far exceed the tax itself.

⚑ Corrected — important  GST on packages is not a "5% or 18%" choice

For a tour operator selling a bundled package, 5% GST without ITC (Input Tax Credit) is mandatory — it is not a free choice against an 18% alternative (CBIC Notif. 11/2017 Entry 23(iii); confirmed by the Delhi AAR TUI India ruling that the 18%-with-ITC option "is not available"). The 18% with ITC figure belongs to a different classification: a travel agent/intermediary earning commission, who pays 18% only on the commission, not on gross package value. So your structure decides your rate — it isn't a slab you pick.

Worked example — why structure matters

A ₹1,00,000 Spiti package, your cost ₹85,000, margin ₹15,000:

  • As tour operator (principal): 5% GST on the gross ₹1,00,000 = ₹5,000, collected from the customer; you cannot reclaim the GST baked into hotels/transport. Sticker price ₹1,05,000.
  • As pure commission agent: 18% GST on your ₹15,000 fee = ₹2,700; the supplier invoices the customer directly. Lighter GST, but you're a facilitator, not the principal.

Domestic packages also went to 5% (no ITC) from 22 Sep 2025. Hotels ₹1,001–7,500/night = 5%; above ₹7,500 = 18%.

The headline numbers ✓ Verified

  • TCS on overseas packages: flat 2% from 1 Apr 2026 (Budget 2026-27), down from the 5%/20% tiers. No threshold — from the first rupee.
  • GST registration: mandatory above ₹20L turnover (₹10L special states).
  • E-invoicing: mandatory once turnover crosses ₹5 crore in any year since FY18.
  • SAC codes: 998552 packages/accommodation; 998551 air-ticket reservation (18%); 998555 tour-operator + visa services (18%); rail agent 9967 (18%).

Direct tax — the part a GST-only checklist forgets

In plain words — TDS, advance tax, and the presumptive scheme

Beyond the GST you collect from customers, you also have obligations when you pay other people. When you pay a DMC (Destination Management Company), a transport operator, or a local guide above certain threshold amounts, the law requires you to deduct a small percentage of their payment and send it directly to the government on their behalf. This is called TDS (Tax Deducted at Source). Missing TDS creates a brutal double penalty: interest on the amount you should have deducted, plus the government disallowing that entire expense when calculating your own taxable profit.

Separately, if your projected total tax bill for the year exceeds ₹10,000, you must pay it in four instalments through the year — this is called advance tax. And a useful simplification for early-stage operators: if your turnover is under ₹2 crore and you operate as a principal (not a commission agent), the presumptive tax scheme (Section 44AD) lets you skip detailed bookkeeping by declaring a fixed percentage of turnover as your taxable profit.

Permits & on-ground (India offbeat)

In plain words — why permits matter and who is responsible for getting them

India restricts entry to certain areas — typically tribal border regions, island territories, and militarily sensitive zones — through a system of travel permits. As a tour operator, understanding this system is not just paperwork: if your traveller cannot enter the destination because the wrong permit was arranged (or not arranged at all), the entire trip collapses and the liability falls on you.

The system runs on two tracks. Indian citizens typically apply online through state government portals — your job is to remind them in advance and give them the correct link and itinerary details. Foreign nationals, on the other hand, almost always need you or your local DMC (Destination Management Company) to formally sponsor their application — meaning your company's name and registration number appear on the permit, making the operator legally responsible for that foreign traveller during their stay in the restricted area.

The key acronyms unpacked: ILP (Inner Line Permit) is what Indian travellers need for protected North-East states like Arunachal Pradesh, Nagaland, Mizoram, and Manipur. PAP (Protected Area Permit) is what foreign nationals need for protected areas. RAP (Restricted Area Permit) is used in Sikkim for certain zones. All foreign PAP and RAP applications are now processed digitally through e-FRRO (Electronic Foreigners Regional Registration Office) at indianfrro.gov.in.

Permits are where offbeat trips go wrong. The rule of thumb: Indian travellers can self-apply almost everywhere online; foreign nationals almost always need you (or a local DMC) to sponsor them. All foreign PAP/RAP are now digital via e-FRRO.
