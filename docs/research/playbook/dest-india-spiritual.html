Why go / vibe

The Char Dham circuit—Yamunotri, Gangotri, Kedarnath, Badrinath—is Hinduism's supreme high-altitude pilgrimage, combining sacred darshan, glacier treks, and raw Himalayan grandeur across four river valleys in a single looping yatra. Every day brings a different ecosystem: hot spring terraces, Bhagirathi gorges, alpine meadows, and the snow-bound peaks above Badrinath that feel like the literal roof of the known world.

Top sights & places within

  • Kedarnath Temple (3,583 m) — one of 12 Jyotirlingas, backdrop of Kedarnath and Kedar Dome peaks
  • Badrinath Temple (3,133 m) — Vishnu's abode on the Alaknanda, flanked by Nar and Narayan peaks
  • Gangotri Temple & Bhagirathi Gorge (3,048 m) — origin of the Ganges ritual, dramatic grey-cliff canyon
  • Yamunotri Temple & Surya Kund hot springs (3,293 m) — sulphurous 88°C pools used to cook prasad rice
  • Mana Village (3,200 m) — last Indian settlement before Tibet; Vyas Gufa, Bhim Pul natural rock bridge
  • Gaumukh Glacier (3,892 m) — true Gangotri source, 18 km trek through moraine and pine; permit required

Activities menu

  • Helicopter transfer to Kedarnath (Phata or Sersi heli-pad; 7-min flight, saves 16 km trek each way)
  • Trek Janki Chatti to Yamunotri Temple (6 km, ~3 hrs, steep but well-maintained mule track)
  • Sunrise aarti and darshan at each shrine — arrive before 6 am to beat 2–3 hr queues in peak May–Jun
  • Ritual dip in Tapt Kund (45°C thermal spring beside Badrinath Temple) before morning puja
  • Gaumukh Glacier day-trek from Gangotri (Forest Dept permit mandatory; 150-person daily cap)
  • Pony or palanquin (doli) hire on the steepest section of Yamunotri and Kedarnath trails

Where to stay

Base nodes with best mid-range picks: Barkot (Yamunotri base) — GMVN Tourist Rest House ₹1,200–2,500/night, basic but clean; Uttarkashi (Gangotri base) — Hotel Alaknanda or similar ₹2,000–4,000/night; Guptkashi/Phata (Kedarnath base) — Kedar Residency or Shivlok camp ₹2,500–5,500/night; Joshimath/Badrinath — Narayan Palace or Sarovar Portico Badrinath ₹3,500–8,000/night. Luxury options exist only at circuit end-points Haridwar and Rishikesh (Ananda, Rishikesh Marriott, ₹12,000–25,000/night). Camping at Kedarnath base camp and Gaumukh route is run by private operators ₹1,500–3,000/tent.

Day trips & nearby pairings

  • Valley of Flowers National Park (UNESCO, 3,658 m) — 13 km from Govindghat near Badrinath; peak Jul–Aug bloom but accessible Sep too
  • Hemkund Sahib (4,329 m) — Sikh shrine, same Govindghat trail fork; combine with Valley of Flowers for 1-night extension
  • Auli ski slopes and Gorson Bugyal — 16 km from Joshimath; cable car viewpoint across Nanda Devi massif in clear weather
  • Harsil valley — 24 km downhill from Gangotri; Wilson Estate, apple orchards, Tibetan-influenced village; good half-day detour

What to pack

Thermal base layers + down jacket, waterproof shell, ankle-support trekking boots, trekking poles, altitude tablets (Diamox 250 mg, start 24 hrs before ascent), ORS sachets, broad-spectrum sunscreen SPF 50, modest temple clothing (full-length, no sleeveless), headlamp, offline map or GPS track, small puja thali with dry prasad (fresh flowers confiscated at entry).

Getting there

  • Air: Jolly Grant Airport (DED), Dehradun — 45 min drive to Rishikesh circuit start; connected to Delhi (45 min), Mumbai, Bangalore. Nearest heli-pads to shrines: Sersi/Phata (Kedarnath), Harsil/Pharsali (Gangotri/Yamunotri), Badrinath town. No scheduled commercial flights to shrine towns.
  • Rail: Haridwar Junction (HW) — 5 hrs from New Delhi on Shatabdi/Nanda Devi Express; Rishikesh station (RISHIKESH) 6 hrs, less frequent. No rail line beyond Rishikesh into Garhwal hills. Pre-book well in advance during Apr–Jun.
  • Road: Delhi → Haridwar 220 km, ~5 hrs (NH-58); Haridwar → Barkot 200 km, ~7 hrs; full road circuit Haridwar–Barkot–Uttarkashi–Guptkashi–Badrinath–Joshimath–Haridwar ~1,100 km, 10–12 days. Shared tempo-travellers and private AC cabs widely available from Haridwar/Rishikesh stands (₹18,000–35,000 per cab for full circuit).

Permits / visa

Indian citizens need no visa or inner-line permit for the four shrine sites; yatra registration is mandatory via registrationandtouristcare.uk.gov.in or at Haridwar/Rishikesh Tourism Department counters — the Char Dham Devasthanam Management Board was abolished in November 2021 and has no current authority — carry the QR-code slip at all shrine check-posts. The Gaumukh glacier trek requires a separate Forest Department permit (₹150/day for Indians, ₹600/day for foreigners), capped at 150 trekkers daily; obtain it at the DFO/Gangotri National Park office in Uttarkashi or the Gangotri forest checkpoint — a district single-window portal (swsuttarkashi.com) exists but is intermittent, so treat in-person as the failsafe and reconfirm online availability before quoting clients. Since September 2024, Gaumukh trekkers must additionally present a registered local guide, proof of trekking insurance, and a medical fitness certificate at the Gangotri National Park checkpoint; groups without a registered guide are turned back even with a valid permit. All Kedarnath helicopter bookings are handled exclusively through IRCTC HeliYatra (heliyatra.irctc.co.in) — there is no operator quota or separate advance-booking channel; the portal opens mid-April (roughly one week before the season), and peak May–June slots sell out within 60–90 minutes of going live. Shrine opening dates are announced annually: the Badrinath-Kedarnath Temple Committee (BKTC) declares Kedarnath and Badrinath dates on Maha Shivratri, 8–10 weeks before opening (15 February 2026 for a 22 April 2026 Kedarnath opening), and the Gangotri-Yamunotri Mandir Samiti sets the other two. NH-7 (Rishikesh–Badrinath corridor, formerly NH-58) and NH-707 (Rishikesh–Barkot–Yamunotri corridor, formerly NH-94) can close without notice July–September due to landslides, with unpredictable BRO clearance times; using the old highway numbers in client materials causes confusion when pilgrims cross-check BRO or NHAI advisories.

Why go / vibe

The world's oldest continuously inhabited city distils 3,000 years of Hindu devotion into 84 ghats along a single crescent of river — every hour, from pre-dawn boat rides to midnight aarti, delivers images no museum can replicate. Pilgrims, philosophers, silk weavers, and the dying converge here in a density of ritual that reframes every traveller's sense of mortality and beauty.

Top sights & places within

  • Dashashwamedh Ghat — nightly Ganga Aarti with seven priests swinging fire over the river
  • Kashi Vishwanath Temple — one of the 12 Jyotirlingas, rebuilt by Ahilyabai Holkar in 1780
  • Manikarnika Ghat — the great cremation ghat burning continuously for centuries
  • Sarnath — 10 km north; deer park where Buddha gave his first sermon; Dhamek Stupa and archaeological museum
  • Ramnagar Fort — 18th-century Kashi Naresh palace across the river; vintage car and arms museum inside
  • Assi Ghat — southernmost literary ghat; sunrise yoga and boat embarkation point

Activities menu

  • Pre-dawn Ganges boat ride past all 84 ghats as the city wakes
  • Evening Ganga Aarti at Dashashwamedh — watch from a boat for unobstructed views
  • Walking tour of old-city gallis: Vishwanath Lane, Thatheri Bazaar, silk weavers quarter Madanpura
  • Sarnath Buddhist circuit — Dhamek Stupa, Chaukhandi, Mulagandha Kuti Vihar, museum
  • Banarasi silk shopping and loom demonstration in Peeli Kothi / Madanpura
  • Sunrise yoga or meditation session at Assi Ghat

Where to stay

Stay within walking distance of the ghats: Assi Ghat area offers the best balance of calm and access — budget guesthouses Rs 800-1,800/night, comfort hotels Rs 3,500-7,000 (Ganges View, Hotel Alka). Godaulia is 5 min from Kashi Vishwanath but extremely noisy. Luxury: BrijRama Palace (heritage haveli on the ghat, Rs 15,000-28,000) or Taj Ganges (Cantonment, quieter, Rs 12,000-20,000).

  • Chunar Fort (40 km / 1 hr) — Mughal-era sandstone fort on the Ganges; easy half-day

Permits / visa

Varanasi falls entirely outside all ILP, PAP, and RAP restricted-area zones; no permit is required for Indian nationals or foreign tourists holding a valid Indian visa. Kashi Vishwanath Temple prohibits all leather items (shoes, belts, bags) — a cloakroom is provided at entry — and bans mobile phones inside the inner sanctum (garbhagriha), though phones are permitted in the outer Dham corridor and complex. Security queues run 45–90 minutes on regular weekdays, 2–3 hours on other weekends, 3–4 hours on Mondays (Somavar, Shiva's sacred day) even in off-peak months, and 4–5+ hours on Mahashivaratri; book fast-track darshan slots in advance at kvtemple.org or the official temple app (confirm the exact app-store listing name before travel). Photography at Manikarnika and Harishchandra cremation ghats carries no formal legal prohibition but is subject to a strict community ban enforced in person by the Dom cremation community; attempting to photograph leads to immediate confrontation and unofficial payment demands — treat it as an absolute ban and stow all phones and cameras before approaching either ghat. Sarnath ASI site entry fees are approximately Rs 40 (Indian nationals) and Rs 600 (foreign nationals), though ASI revised rates across centrally protected monuments in 2024 and the foreign-national fee may now be Rs 800 or higher — reconfirm at the gate or at asi.nic.in before quoting clients. The Dhamekh Stupa archaeological zone and the Sarnath Archaeological Museum are separately ticketed.

Why go / vibe

Haridwar is one of Hinduism's seven sacred cities — its nightly Ganga Aarti at Har Ki Pauri is among the most visceral spectacles in India. Rishikesh, 25 km upstream, layers that spiritual gravity with serious adventure: the same river that receives flower-and-flame offerings at dusk launches Grade III–IV rafts at dawn.

Top sights & places within

  • Har Ki Pauri ghat, Haridwar — premier aarti site, submerged clock-tower marks the exact spot where Ganga leaves the mountains
  • Triveni Ghat, Rishikesh — evening aarti on a wider bend, less crowded than Haridwar, excellent for photography
  • Laxman Jhula & Ram Jhula — twin colonial-era suspension bridges framing the Ganga gorge, lined with cafés and ashrams
  • Beatles Ashram (Chaurasi Kutia) — Maharishi Mahesh Yogi's ruined forest retreat where the Fab Four wrote the White Album; open to visitors
  • Neelkanth Mahadev Temple — 12th-century Shiva shrine at 1,330 m, 12 km from Rishikesh, set in sal forest
  • Rajaji National Park — 820 sq km reserve straddling Haridwar; elephants, leopards, gharial; accessible November–June

Activities menu

  • White-water rafting — Shivpuri to Rishikesh (16 km, Grade II–III, ~2.5 hr) or Kaudiyala to Rishikesh (35 km, Grade III–IV, full day)
  • Bungee jumping at Jumpin Heights, Mohan Chatti — India's highest fixed-platform bungee at 83 m over the Ganga
  • Yoga and meditation retreat — 100+ AYUSH-registered ashrams; single-day drop-in classes from ₹300 or residential packages from ₹1,200/day
  • Rajaji National Park jeep safari (dawn slot) — elephant herds most visible November–March near Chilla range
  • Riverside camping and kayaking at Shivpuri or Byasi — basic to boutique camps on Ganga gravel beaches
  • Trekking to Neelkanth Mahadev — 12 km forested trail from Laxman Jhula; 4–5 hr return; good birding en route

Getting there

Permits / visa

No inner-line permit or protected-area permit is required for Indian nationals; Kerala is not an ILP state and Sabarimala falls outside any restricted zone under the Foreigners Protected Areas Order. All visitors must pre-book a VQ (Virtual Queue) darshan slot at sabarimalaonline.org; during Mandalam and Makaravilakku peak seasons, slots release approximately 10–15 days before the intended darshan date — for the 2025 Mandalam season opening 16 November, booking went live 1 November and December slots opened 24 November — so monitor the portal and have clients ready to book the moment slots appear, as popular dates sell out within hours of release. Pilgrims observing a 41-day vrat must wear maala (rudraksha or tulsi beads) and carry irumudi kettu; both are verified at checkpoints before entry is permitted. Entry for women aged 10–50 remains de facto restricted at the shrine despite the 2018 Supreme Court ruling; a 9-judge Constitution Bench re-hearing was ongoing as of April 2026 with no final ruling reversing the ground-level ban — reconfirm current state practice before booking female clients in that age range. The temple opens for Mandalam (Mandalapooja Mahotsavam) on Vrishchikam 1 of the Malayalam calendar, a date that falls approximately 14–17 November in the Gregorian calendar (16 November in 2025), and closes around 27 December on Mandalapooja day; Makaravilakku Mahotsavam is a multi-week season running approximately 30 December to 20 January, with 14 January being specifically Makaravilakku Day when the Makarajyoti celestial event occurs. The temple also opens on the first five days of each Malayalam month and is closed all other periods; confirm exact Malayalam calendar dates each year as Gregorian equivalents shift annually.
