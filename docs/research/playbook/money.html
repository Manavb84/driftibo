How travel agents actually make money

This chapter shows you exactly where each rupee of margin comes from, how the timing of money flowing in versus money flowing out works, and the honest, legal levers that let a boutique agency earn well — even without airline commissions or a big sales team.

12

Money mechanics

In plain words: the core cash-flow problem of this business

Imagine you are a middleman who buys from a wholesale market and sells at retail. The wholesale market (your suppliers — hotels, jeep operators, guides) demands payment weeks before the product is delivered. Your customer, meanwhile, often pays you in stages. And if you earn a commission, the hotel sends you that payment after the guest has already checked out and gone home.

This means you are regularly bridging a gap: you owe suppliers money before the trip happens, but you haven't been fully paid yet. The playbook fix is simple: collect from customers in the right stages, matched to when your suppliers need to be paid — and keep a separate cash reserve so you're never borrowing from one customer to pay another supplier.

The core cash-flow problem of this business: you pay suppliers before travel and get paid commission after — so you bridge it with customer advances and a reserve. Design your collection schedule to match your supplier deadlines.

How the money actually moves — a step-by-step walk-through

Here is the full journey of a real booking, from enquiry to final settlement, so you can see exactly who holds the money at each stage and where the risk sits.

  1. Day 1 — Customer books. A traveller pays you a 25–30% advance (say ₹8,750 on a ₹35,000 package). You have the cash; the trip is 90 days away.
  2. Day 1–2 — Supplier deposit due. Your DMC or hotel wants 30–50% of their fee within 48 hours to lock the booking. You pay it from the customer advance you just collected.
  3. Day 30 — 60 days before travel. You collect the next instalment from the customer (another 50% of the package price). Your supplier's balance payment — typically the remaining amount — is due 45–60 days before travel. These two deadlines should line up: what you collect covers what you owe.
  4. Day 60 — 30 days before travel. You collect the final balance from the customer (100% collected). You confirm all supplier bookings. The money is now fully in — the risk of a shortfall is behind you.
  5. During travel. The DMC runs the trip. Your vouchers (the booking confirmation documents you issue to the customer) are the proof that the customer has paid for each service.
  6. After checkout — commission arrives (if applicable). If you booked a hotel on a commission basis rather than a net rate, the hotel sends your commission 30–45 days after checkout. This is why commission-only agencies have chronic cash-flow strain: the money arrives weeks after you needed it.
  7. The better model: net-rate deals. If instead you bought the hotel at a net rate and sold it at the retail price, you already collected the full margin when the customer paid — no waiting 30–45 days post-checkout for a commission cheque.
Why the collection schedule matters

If travel is within 14 days of booking, collect 100% upfront — you have no time to collect in stages, and cancellation risk is at its highest. For bookings further out, stagger it: 25–30% at booking, 50% at 60 days out, balance at 30 days. This keeps your cash ahead of your supplier deadlines at every stage.

The timing mismatch (and how to bridge it)

  • Suppliers want: 30–50% deposit within 48h, balance 45–60 days before travel.
  • Hotel commission arrives: 30–45 days after checkout.
  • So collect from customers: 25–30% at booking → 50% at 60 days out → balance at 30 days; 100% upfront if travel is within 14 days.
  • Keep a reserve: 2–3 months of operating expenses, separate from client funds.

Margins to plan around

  • Hotels on commission: 10–15%; transfers 15–20%; tours/activities 15–25%.
  • Net-rate markup (mid-range): 20–30%.
  • Target gross 18–25% → net profit 8–15% after overheads.
  • Bespoke offbeat bundles (your edge): 15–25%+.

Getting paid by your customers — payment gateways

A payment gateway is the software layer that processes card payments, UPI transfers, and net banking on your website or WhatsApp checkout link. The gateway charges you a percentage of every transaction — called the MDR — plus the 18% GST on that fee.

Cancellation, refunds & advances

Your cancellation terms are both a legal document and a trust signal. The rules below are grounded in the Consumer Protection Act 2019 (CPA 2019) — the Indian law that protects buyers and penalises unfair terms.

  • Land packages — a defensible slab to paste into your T&C (synthesised from 9 Indian trek operators + CPA 2019): the customer always forfeits the 25% non-refundable booking fee; the remaining refund depends on how far out they cancel:

    30 or more days75%
    15–29 days50%

    One free reschedule if 30+ days out. Refund to the original method within ~10 working days. Many operators offer a higher % as a 12-month voucher than as cash — legal if disclosed before payment. (A voucher is a future-booking credit rather than a cash refund.)
  • Keep it proportionate. CPA 2019 §49 voids a penalty "wholly disproportionate to the loss" — so size the 25% booking fee to your documented advance supplier payments per booking, and keep that cost sheet. The E-Commerce Rules also require symmetry (you bear a charge if you cancel).
  • Force majeure → a full-value voucher, not cash. Force majeure means an event outside anyone's control that makes the trip impossible. Spell out the Himalayan triggers explicitly: landslide/flood/avalanche/heavy snow closing the route; road-closure or NDMA/State-DMA advisory; shrine/park closure by the authority; bandh/curfew/terrorism; declared health emergency. Issue a 100% travel credit (less documented non-recoverable costs), valid 12 months, family-transferable — the consumer-friendly, court-safe standard.
  • Flights: DGCA's free 48-hour cancel window (from 26 Mar 2026) applies to direct airline bookings ≥7 days out (domestic) / 15 (intl) — say in your T&Cs whether you pass it through.
  • No escrow is mandatory for non-IATA agents in India — but under the Consumer Protection Act 2019, a refund delayed beyond ~60 days is "deficiency in service", and a customer can sue you in their city. Refund to the original payment method, fast.

The margin levers: where every rupee of profit comes from

"Commission" is the smallest part of how a good agency earns. The real money is in buying wholesale and selling retail, then stacking small extras on top. Here's where every rupee of margin actually comes from — and the honest, legal way to capture it.

The one idea that changes everything: net-rate arbitrage

Think of it like a grocery wholesaler vs. a corner shop. The corner shop charges customers ₹60 for a bottle of oil. The wholesaler supplies that same bottle to the shop at ₹45. The shop's ₹15 profit is the markup. In travel, you are the corner shop. If a hotel charges customers ₹5,000/night but gives travel agents a net rate of ₹4,000/night, your markup is ₹1,000 — a 25% gross margin.

Compare that to earning a commission: if the hotel listed at ₹5,000/night and paid you 10–12% commission, you'd earn ₹500–600/night — roughly half what the net-rate model gives you. Earning 10–12% commission on a ₹40,000 booking pays you ₹4,000–4,800. Buying that same inventory at a net rate 15–25% below retail and selling at retail pays you ₹8,000–10,000 (a 20–33% margin) — roughly 2–3× more per rupee of GMV. Offbeat destinations (Chopta, Spiti, Gokarna) have no public price to compare against, so the markup holds. This single lever is what funds an organic content business with no outside capital.

How net-rate arbitrage actually works — the full journey of a deal

A concrete example: Chopta 3-night package

Imagine a traveller wants a 3-night stay in Chopta with jeep transfers, a guided Tungnath trek, and packed meals.

  1. You contact your DMC in Chopta. This is a local operator who has already negotiated seasonal rates with three homestays, employs two licensed guides, and owns a Bolero for transfers. They quote you a net rate of ₹22,000 per person for the 3-night package — this is the wholesale price. It is not visible to the public.
  2. You build your selling price. You add a markup of ~27% and list the package on your website at ₹28,000 per person. Your gross margin is ₹6,000 per person. If you sell five spots in a group, that's ₹30,000 gross profit on one trip — without having hired a single guide or owned a jeep yourself.
  3. Customer books and pays. The traveller pays you ₹28,000. You keep your ₹6,000 margin and pay the DMC ₹22,000 on their schedule (deposit first, balance before travel).
  4. The DMC runs the trip. You issue the customer a voucher — a document proving they've paid. The DMC accepts the voucher and runs the accommodation, guide, and transfers.
  5. After the trip. If the trip went well, the DMC is happy (they kept their ₹22,000 and your volume helps them fill their homestay all season). You are happy (₹6,000 margin per head, no ground operations to manage). The traveller got a curated, smooth experience at a price they couldn't have found on Google.

Why does the DMC need you? The DMC is not a marketer. They are experts at running on-ground operations — building routes, managing guides, keeping vehicles on mountain roads. They rely on agents like you to fill their capacity. Without you, their homestay sits half-empty. So they give you a net rate lower than retail, because a guaranteed booking from you is worth more than a maybe-booking from a random traveller.

The ancillary stack, in rupees

Ancillaries are the small add-ons you offer at checkout — each one earns a small margin at nearly zero extra effort. Stacked together, they meaningfully improve your total margin per booking.

  • Travel insurance (IRDAI POSP): ~10–15% of premium (the EOM cap — the maximum commission the insurer is allowed to pay) — ₹50–75 on a ₹500 domestic policy, ₹200–300 on a ₹2,000 intl policy. (The "20–40%" figure floating around is cruise-market, not Indian POSP.)
  • Guide/porter upsell: contract at ₹900/day, charge ₹1,500–2,000 — 50–100% markup.
  • Helicopter/charter upsell: 10–15% referral, or your own markup as a broker (§5).
  • Camp/tent upgrade: you keep the full standard→glamping difference.
  • Visa service fee: ₹500–5,000/applicant over the actual fee (§15).
  • Forex spread: 2–3% — only relevant once trips go cross-border. (Forex spread is the gap between what you pay for foreign currency and what you charge the customer.)

Stacked on a ₹35,000 package, ancillaries add ₹1,500–3,000/head ≈ 4–8 margin points.

The compounding levers (where it gets cheap to grow)

These levers do not add margin directly — they reduce what you spend to earn each rupee of revenue.

  • Repeat: adventure customers rebook at an estimated 35–40% within 18 months. (industry inference, not bucket-list-specific) A customer who comes back costs you nearly nothing to acquire again.
  • Referral: referred customers carry ~16% higher LTV, 37% lower churn (they stay loyal longer), and a sub-₹1,000 effective cost vs ₹3,000–5,000 for a paid ad.
  • Float funds growth: deposits collected 45–60 days out, suppliers paid 30 days out — the gap (the float) pays your tooling bill.
  • GST is a real cost line: on the 5% package slab you can't reclaim input GST — price it in, don't forget it.
⊕ For Driftibo

Your whole margin architecture rests on net-rate DMC contracts, not commission — without them you're a 10–12% reseller and the numbers in §16 don't close. Stack insurance + guide + heli + camp-upgrade as one-tap add-ons in the WhatsApp flow (worth ₹35–50 lakh/yr at scale, near-zero effort). Treat the deposit float as your working-capital engine, and keep cancellation tiers disclosed and proportionate so the fee income stays revenue, not a CCPA liability.

Risk & trust

In plain words: the two sides of risk

Running a travel agency means carrying two kinds of risk. First, the risk that you cause harm — a customer buys a policy you weren't authorised to sell, or gets injured on a transfer you arranged, and you're liable. Second, the risk that someone harms you — a fake supplier takes your deposit, or a customer disputes their card payment after travelling. Both are manageable with the right licences, defences, and paperwork. This section shows you exactly what to put in place.

Selling travel insurance — the legal route ✓ Verified

You can't just resell insurance policies — you need authorisation from IRDAI. Three legal routes, in rising order of cost and capability:

  • POSP (Point of Sales Person): a person (you or staff) does 15 hrs training + exam and is appointed under a licensed sponsor — note POSPs aren't directly "IRDAI-registered"; the sponsor holds the licence. Tied to one insurer. Travel insurance is an approved POSP product (sum insured up to ₹3 cr).
  • Corporate Agent: the agency itself registers with IRDAI (~₹50L net worth, a Principal Officer with 50-hr training) and can tie up with up to 9 general insurers. IRDAI's own portal confirms "a travel agent may offer travel insurance as a corporate agent."
  • Broker: full registration, broadest product access (higher capital + PI cover).

Commission: no fixed caps since Apr 2024 (set within the insurer's EOM limit). 2026: registrations are now perpetual (annual fee + compliance) rather than fixed 3-year terms.

Carrying your own liability

When you arrange a trip, you can be legally responsible if something goes wrong — even if the supplier caused the problem. Two policies protect you:

  • Professional Indemnity (PI) — covers errors, negligent advice, defence costs (premium ~0.5–1.5% of cover; claims-made, so buy tail cover when winding a season down — meaning you need the policy active even after the season ends to cover claims that arrive late).
  • Commercial General Liability (CGL) — bodily injury on transfers, boat/animal rides, adventure activities.
  • Indian indemnity caps reach ₹35 crore; PI+CGL together is the standard tour-operator structure.

Supplier & payment fraud — the patterns to refuse

Fraud in travel comes from two directions: fake suppliers who take your money, and dishonest customers who dispute payments after travelling. Here is what to watch for and how to block it.

What scammers do

  • Fake DMCs/wholesalers take your deposit for inventory they don't control. You pay; the hotel has never heard of them; your customer arrives to find no booking.
  • Cloned hotel/OTA sites (e.g. "booking-deals.com") collect for non-existent rooms — peaks around Char Dham/Haj & long weekends.
  • Customer chargeback abuse: book on card, travel, then dispute as "unauthorised" — effectively getting a free trip.
  • Phishing/BEC: fake IATA/BSP invoices to a spoofed account.

Your defences

  • Verify suppliers on IATAN CheckACode (IATAN is IATA's accreditation database); insist on direct-to-property confirmation codes so you can verify the booking exists at the hotel itself.
  • Never pay suppliers by Western Union/crypto; call a known number to confirm wire details before any transfer.
  • Keep signed booking agreements, vouchers, PNRs — your evidence in a chargeback (you have a 90-day window to submit your counter-evidence).
  • Take big payments by card/bank transfer (reversible) over UPI.

Trust & the law you must publish

In India, the Consumer Protection Act 2019 and the IT Rules 2021 impose specific obligations on anyone selling services online. Here is what you must have visible on your website and in your booking documents.

  • Terms & Conditions must state, in plain language: price breakdown, cancellation tiers + deadlines, refund timelines, force majeure, and a dispute/grievance path.
  • Grievance Officer (IT Rules 2021): publish contact details for a named person who handles complaints; acknowledge in 24h, resolve in 15 days, if you run a site/app. This is a legal requirement, not optional.
  • Don't over-charge cancellation: the CCPA is (May 2026) probing OTAs charging cancellation fees far above the airline's actual fee — fees above the supplier's real levy are an "unfair trade practice" under the law.
  • Refunds go back to the original payment instrument (i.e. if they paid by credit card, refund to the same credit card); RBI's Payment Aggregator rules + CPA 2019 both bite if you stall.
