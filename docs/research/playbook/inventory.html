Chapter 1 · Driftibo Operator Playbook

Getting inventory — what to sell without signing hotel deals

This chapter shows you, in plain language, exactly how a travel operator sources hotel rooms, packages and experiences to sell — without needing direct contracts with individual hotels — and walks you through the complete real-money journey of a deal, from the first search to the moment your traveller checks in.

01

The two ways to get inventory without tie-ups

In plain words — what "getting inventory" means for a travel operator

Before you can sell someone a trip to Chopta or Spiti, you need something concrete to sell them: a hotel bed, a shared jeep, a local guide, a permit. Taken together, everything you can book and resell is called your inventory. The challenge every new operator faces is: how do you get access to that inventory when you haven't signed deals with hundreds of individual hotels?

The answer is that two types of wholesale middlemen have already done all that contracting for you. Think of it like this: you're opening a corner grocery store. Instead of driving personally to every farm in the country, you buy from two kinds of supplier. The first is a giant national cash-and-carry wholesaler with every product imaginable — you walk in, pick what you need off the shelf, pay the wholesale price, and add your margin. The second is a specialist local distributor who knows every farmer in one particular region, sells you pre-made bundles of the best local produce, and handles all the logistics. The first is a bedbank. The second is a DMC.

You do not need to sign deals with hotels to sell rooms and packages. Two B2B channels already did that work for you. Understanding how each actually moves money and risk is the foundation for everything else.

How a deal actually works, start to finish

This is the step-by-step journey of a real inventory transaction — who the players are, how the money moves, and who carries the risk at each moment. Read this once and the rest of the chapter will make complete sense.

The bedbank journey (example: booking a city hotel via TBO Holidays)

Step 1 — Find: You log into the B2B portal of a bedbank like TBO Holidays. You search "Rishikesh, 2 nights, 2 adults, 15 Sept." Hundreds of hotels appear instantly. The prices you see are the net rates — the wholesale price the bedbank passes to you. They are 15–40% below what your customer would pay on a public booking site.

Step 2 — Quote: You note the net rate (say, ₹4,000/night). This is what you will pay. The customer never sees this number.

Step 3 — Add your markup: You decide how much profit to add. This is called your markup. You add 25%, so you quote the customer ₹5,000/night. Your gross margin on this one room is ₹1,000/night.

Step 4 — Confirm and book: Your customer says yes and pays you ₹10,000 (2 nights). You go back to the portal and click "Book." The booking is confirmed instantly — no waiting, no negotiation. The portal draws ₹8,000 (your net cost) either from a prepaid wallet you have already funded, or from a credit line the bedbank has extended to you (backed by a cash deposit or a bank guarantee you provided when you joined).

Step 5 — Issue the voucher: Within seconds, the portal generates a voucher — a PDF you hand to your traveller. It shows the hotel name, dates, room type and a reference number. Your customer shows it at check-in. They never see what you paid.

Step 6 — Pay and risk: You have already paid (via your funded wallet or credit line). You carry all the cancellation risk. If your customer cancels 10 days before check-in, the hotel's penalty (often 100% inside 15 days) is deducted from your wallet. Your customer owes you that money — how you manage that is covered in the cancellation policy chapter. The bedbank does not absorb this; it is your problem.

The DMC journey (example: booking a 4-night Spiti valley circuit)

Step 1 — Find: You discover a DMC called Mountain Mysteries that specialises in Spiti and Kinnaur in Himachal Pradesh. Unlike a bedbank, this DMC does not have an instant self-serve portal. You reach out via their B2B enquiry form or WhatsApp with your client's dates and requirements.

Step 2 — Quote: Within a few hours to a day, the DMC sends you a detailed itinerary with a net rate quote — say, ₹18,000 per person for a 4-night package covering stays at homestays, a shared SUV, a guide, and all park entry fees. You cannot find this in any bedbank; these properties and permits simply do not exist there.

Step 3 — Add your markup: You add your markup — perhaps 20% — and quote your customer ₹21,600 per person. Your gross margin is ₹3,600 per person.

Step 4 — Confirm and pay the deposit: Your customer pays you. You go back to the DMC and say "confirmed." The DMC immediately asks for a 20–30% deposit of the total net cost to hold the booking. You wire this now. At this point, this deposit is non-refundable if your client later cancels. The balance (the remaining 70–80%) is due 45–60 days before the travel date.

Step 5 — Issue the voucher: Once you have paid the deposit, the DMC issues a voucher — typically on white-label paper in your agency's name, not the DMC's. Your traveller never knows which DMC you used.

Step 6 — Pay and risk: You pay the deposit immediately and the balance 45–60 days before travel. Who carries what risk? The DMC absorbs operational failures — if the jeep doesn't show up, if the guesthouse is overbooked, if a guide falls sick, the DMC must find a replacement at their own cost. But if your client cancels, your deposit is lost. This is the fundamental trade: operational reliability in exchange for committed cash upfront.

Bedbank vs DMC — the one-sentence difference

A bedbank is like Amazon — an enormous catalogue, instant price and confirmation, standardised products you can find anywhere. A DMC is like a master tailor who has spent 20 years living in one valley — they cannot sell you a Marriott, but they can sell you a hand-built experience that does not exist on any public website.

Route A

Bedbanks & B2B portals

Wholesalers (TBO, Hotelbeds, RezLive…) buy hotel rooms in bulk at net rates and resell them to any registered travel business through a login or an API. You never contract the hotel.

  • How you join: register a business + GST/PAN, get approved in 24h–few days (some, like Hotelbeds, take 2–6 weeks).
  • Access: web portal for manual booking, or XML/JSON API to wire into your own site.
  • You pay by: prepaid wallet / deposit, or "book now, pay later" credit (a deposit or bank guarantee backs your credit line).
  • Voucher: issued instantly at confirmation, on the bedbank's paper (often white-label-able).
  • Who carries the risk: you do. Once confirmed, a no-show is treated as a late cancel — you can owe from one night up to 100% of the booking.
  • Margin: net rates run 15–40% below public price; you set a markup; effective gross ~12–28%.
Route B

DMCs & ground handlers

A Destination Management Company is a local specialist that has contracted the hotels, jeeps, guides and permits in its region and sells you the bundle at net rates via a B2B agreement.

  • How you join: a bilateral B2B agreement; show your credentials (IATO/ADTOI/IATA or just GST) + your volume in that region; approved in 1–2 days.
  • Access: portal login or quote-by-email/WhatsApp; the DMC builds the itinerary.
  • You pay by: 20–30% deposit to confirm, balance 45–60 days before travel (net-15/30 credit comes after you build a track record).
  • Voucher: issued at confirmation, usually white-labelled in your agency's name.
  • Who carries the risk: the DMC absorbs operational failure (overbooking, a no-show driver — it must fix it), but your deposit is non-refundable if the client cancels.
  • Margin: 8–18% on resort+transfer packages; 25–45% on bespoke / luxury itineraries.

03

Offbeat sourcing — your real supply chain

In plain words — why offbeat destinations require a different approach

A village homestay in Spiti Valley, a campsite above Chopta, a trek into Ziro in Arunachal — these places are Driftibo's entire selling point. The problem: they simply do not appear on any bedbank. Hotelbeds cannot book you a room in a Himalayan cooperative guesthouse that has no website and pays no commission. So your real supply chain for the experiences that define your brand runs through a completely different set of partners: regional DMCs, community cooperatives, and state government homestay registries.

The upside: once you have these relationships, your product becomes very hard for a competitor to replicate by clicking around on TBO.

The places that look like abroad but aren't are exactly the ones bedbanks don't carry. Here you buy from regional DMCs, community cooperatives and state homestay registries — and you vet them like a pro, because your deposit and your reputation ride on them.

How to find & vet a regional DMC

Vetting checklist (run before you wire a rupee)

  • Check Ministry of Tourism (MoT) recognition on etraveltradeapproval.nic.in
  • Confirm IATO / ADTOI listing (and ATOAI for trekking/adventure)
  • Validate the GST number; ask for a liability/indemnity COI naming you
  • Get 2+ agent references and a direct-to-property confirmation, not just the DMC's word
  • Red flags: Western Union/crypto/UPI-only requests, no track record, vague address

Contract terms to negotiate

  • Rate type: FIT (per-traveller net) vs group (10+ pax, typically 10–30% cheaper)
  • Allotment: hard (20–35% off but you pay even if unsold) · soft/free-sale with a 14–21 day release (10–25% off, lower risk) · on-request (5–15% off, zero risk)
  • Payment: aim 20–25% deposit + balance 7–14 days pre-arrival; offbeat/trek DMCs may want balance 60 days out
  • Margin reality: DMC packages give you ~8–18%; your own bundled experiences can reach 15–25%+
What "allotment" means in plain language

When a DMC offers you allotment, it is giving you the right to hold a block of rooms or vehicle seats without committing to sell all of them immediately. There are three types, with very different risk profiles:

  • Hard allotment — the DMC blocks 5 rooms for you for the whole of October. You get a steep 20–35% discount. But you must pay for all 5 rooms whether you sell them or not. This is only worth it when you are confident you will fill them.
  • Soft allotment (free-sale with release) — the DMC holds the rooms for you, but if you haven't confirmed a booking by 14–21 days before the travel date, they are automatically released back and can be sold to someone else. You get a 10–25% discount with no penalty for rooms you don't sell. This is the sensible starting point while you are building your sales pipeline.
  • On-request — no rooms are held at all. When a customer books with you, you check with the DMC in real time. You get only 5–15% off, but carry zero risk of paying for empty rooms. Best for destinations you sell rarely.
