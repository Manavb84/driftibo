Chapter 2 · Driftibo Operator Playbook

Moving people — flights, charter and ground transport

This chapter tells you exactly how to sell flights, helicopters and private jets without owning a single aircraft, and how to source ground vehicles in the mountains without paying city prices — covering every lever, player and money flow from the moment a traveller says yes.

04

Flights & getting fares cheaper

In plain words — how flight-selling actually works for a new travel company

To issue an airline ticket yourself you normally need an IATA accreditation number. Getting your own IATA number takes time, capital and a year or more in business. But here is the shortcut the industry actually uses: a B2B portal already has its own IATA number and lets you search, book and issue tickets through its account. You sign up, load a prepaid wallet, search for the flight, pay, and a valid ticket comes out — all without your own IATA. The portal handles the airline's money pipe invisibly in the background. You collect the customer's fare and keep the difference. Think of it like selling a concert ticket through Paytm: the venue does not care who issued the ticket, and you don't need to be the venue.

You can sell flights from day one without IATA — a B2B portal's own accreditation issues the ticket. IATA is an upgrade you grow into, not a starting requirement. Beyond the basics, this section also explains the part most founders miss: how to get the same seat cheaper for your customer — and keep the difference.

Getting the same seat cheaper — in plain words

The wholesale layer under the price on Google Flights

The fare you see on Google Flights is the retail price. Underneath it sits a wholesale layer that most travellers and many new agents never know about. Here are the three main doors into it:

Door 1 — the consolidator: A company that pre-buys seats in bulk from airlines at secret "net" fares (30–60% below retail for economy; premium cabins can save even more). It then resells those seats to you, the agent, at a price still below what the public pays. You add your markup, quote your client one price, and keep the spread. The consolidator uses its own IATA to issue the ticket — you never need yours.

Door 2 — group / series fares: Go to the airline's group desk with 10 or more passengers and ask for a block. They hold a set of seats on a specific date at a negotiated price, with name-change flexibility that public fares do not have. IndiGo, Air India and Akasa all have dedicated group portals.

Door 3 — NDC: The airline's modern direct feed, which carries fare bundles (seat + bag + meal) and special prices that the old GDS computer screens cannot show. Not the deepest discount, but richer offers. Air India was India's first NDC airline in 2024; globally about 24% of indirect ticket sales ran through NDC by 2026.

How a ticket actually gets issued "for you" — the invisible plumbing

You register on a B2B portal (Riya, TBO, Agent Bazar…). The portal holds the IATA number and the BSP settlement account — the airline's money pipe. You search for the flight, pay from a prepaid wallet or credit line, and the portal issues the ticket in its own name. You collect the customer's fare and keep the spread between your selling price and the net fare. BSP settlement happens invisibly between the portal and the airline. At no point do you need your own IATA.

The package-bundling GST angle

Sell a flight on its own and your commission is taxed at 18% GST (air-agent service, SAC 998551). Fold the same flight into a tour package (flight + accommodation + transport, one invoice) and the whole bundle sits on the 5% tour-operator slab (no ITC), effective from 22 September 2025. For the customer it is one clean all-in price; for you it is simpler invoicing — see §7 and §10.

⊕ For Driftibo

Skip IATA entirely for now. Sell flights through TBO or Riya Connect and add a free TIDS code for supplier recognition. Most of your trips are domestic and DMC-driven anyway; revisit GoLite only when direct airline commissions and PLBs would beat the portal markup.

05

Charter flights — with zero of your own capital

Helicopters and private jets sound like something only an airline can sell. They are not. You sell them the same way you sell a hotel room you do not own — as a broker. You never buy an aircraft, never hold a flying licence, never carry the operational risk.

In plain words — what a charter broker actually is (an everyday analogy)

Think of a real-estate agent. They do not own the house. They know which houses are available, introduce the buyer to the seller, help negotiate a price, and earn a fee on the deal. A charter broker does exactly the same thing with aircraft. The aircraft operator owns or leases the helicopter or jet and holds a NSOP (Non-Scheduled Operator Permit) from the DGCA (Directorate General of Civil Aviation). Getting that permit, maintaining the aircraft, employing the pilots — all of that is the operator's burden and their cost. You, the broker, simply know who those operators are and what routes they fly. When a traveller asks you for a helicopter, you call 2–3 operators, collect quotes, pick the best, add your margin, and quote one price to your client. The whole legal framework (DGCA Aircraft Rules 1937, Rule 134A) binds the aircraft operator — not the person selling the seat. All you need is your normal business registration and a GST number. ✓ Verified

How the whole deal works, start to finish — in plain words

The zero-capital charter flow: who the players are, where the money goes, step by step

Here is the exact journey of a real Himalayan helicopter deal. Four players: you (Driftibo, the tour operator acting as broker), a wholesale charter broker (optional middleman — a specialist like Air Charters India who aggregates many operators), the helicopter operator (the company that holds the NSOP and owns the aircraft), and your traveller (the client paying for the trip).

Step 1 — the traveller asks you for a helicopter leg. Say your client wants to fly from Manali to Kaza in Spiti Valley — a drive that takes 7–9 hours over high-altitude mountain roads. They are happy to pay extra for speed and the experience. You now have a booking to source.

Step 2 — you call the operators (or a broker). You have two options here. You can call helicopter operators directly — companies like Air Himalayas (based in Manali, closest to Spiti) or Aone Helicopters — and ask for a charter quote. Or, if you have no existing operator relationships, you call a wholesale charter broker like Air Charters India. The broker has relationships with dozens of operators across India and sources the best-fit aircraft for your route, taking their own 5–10% cut from the operator side. Either way, you end up with a quote: say the operator or broker quotes you ₹5,50,000 for the full aircraft (the AS350 B3 helicopter, 5 passenger seats, Manali–Kaza return, roughly 3 flying hours).

Step 3 — you add your markup and quote the client one price. You decide to add 12% on top of the ₹5,50,000 quote. Your selling price to the client is ₹6,16,000 (plus 18% GST on the charter, since private helicopter charter sits on the 18% slab — HSN 9964). You quote the client this all-in number. If you fold the helicopter leg into a full tour package invoice (trip + stay + helicopter), the package rides the 5% tour-operator GST slab instead — see §10 for detail.

Step 4 — the client says yes and pays a deposit. Helicopter operators need roughly 50% of the charter cost to confirm the booking. But you collect 25–50% from your client first, before you pay the operator a single rupee. This is the key to the zero-capital model: your client's money arrives in your account before you commit any of your own. Lead time is 7–10 days for a standard booking; emergency bookings in 3 hours are possible at a premium.

Step 5 — you pay the operator's deposit from the client's money. You pass ~50% of the operator's quoted cost to the operator to lock the date and aircraft. You have not put any of your own money at risk — the client's deposit covered it. The remaining client balance is collected 7–15 days before the flight; you pay the operator's balance at a similar point.

Step 6 — the helicopter flies. On the day, the operator's crew and aircraft do the work. You are not on site. The traveller lands in Kaza.

Step 7 — you keep the spread. You collected ₹6,16,000 from the client (plus GST). You paid the operator ₹5,50,000. Your gross margin on this leg is ₹66,000 — about 12% of the charter cost — earned with no aircraft, no pilot, no NSOP, and none of your own capital at risk.

Who takes what — the money split

  • A pure broker typically earns 5–10% of the charter cost (real-world range runs wider, ~3–20% by deal). ✓ Verified
  • You, reselling as a tour operator, add your own markup on top of the broker or operator quote — 10–15% is normal for a leisure package.
  • GST: private charter sits on 18% (HSN 9964); the government-run pilgrimage shuttle to Kedarnath is 5%. Fold charter into a tour package and the whole bill rides the 5% tour-operator slab.
  • Deposit flow: operators want ~50% to confirm; collect 25–50% from your client first, balance 7–15 days out. Lead time 7–10 days (3 hours possible at a premium).

Empty legs — the cheap-seat hack

When an aircraft drops a group at their destination and has to fly back empty to its base, the operator has already paid for that return flight in fuel and crew time whether anyone is on board or not. So they sell that "empty leg" at a steep discount — just to recover some of the cost.

Private jets and fixed-wing aircraft — for premium groups

The same broker model applies. India had 121 charter aircraft and 133 NSOP operators by mid-2025 (the largest charter fleet in Asia-Pacific) — you need none of that to sell charters. A private jet upsell only makes financial sense for a group of 5–8 people heading to a destination with limited commercial service (Leh, Srinagar, Bhuntar for Spiti, Pakyong for Sikkim) and where the client is already spending ₹3–5L per person. Below 4 passengers, a business class commercial flight plus a premium cab is usually cheaper.

Local ground transport & transfers

Moving people does not end when a flight lands or a helicopter touches down — it ends when the traveller walks through the property door. This section covers the ground leg of that journey: the transfer from the airport or helipad to the destination, which on Himalayan routes is often the costliest, most logistically tangled stretch of the whole trip.

In plain words — why ground transport is the trickiest line in your cost sheet

Transport is typically 40–50% of an offbeat trip's total cost, and it is the lowest-margin line in the travel business — meaning there is less room to mark it up without your package becoming uncompetitively expensive. So where you source the vehicle matters enormously. The three sourcing options below sound similar on paper but behave very differently in the mountains: one of them (self-drive apps) barely works there at all, and one (the local taxi union) is the only legal option in places like Ladakh. Get this right and your package is profitable; get it wrong and you are subsidising your customers' transport with your own margin.

Transport is 40–50% of an offbeat trip's cost and the lowest-margin line in travel — so where you source it decides whether a package makes money. There are three ways to get a vehicle, and on mountain routes only two of them actually work.

1 · B2B aggregator portals

Book transfers and cabs through a portal at net rates, then set your own markup.

  • TBO Transfers — free signup, markup model, good for airport and city transfer legs
  • Savaari via MMT myPartner — outstation cabs, 2,000+ cities, commission model
  • RezLive — hotel + transfer + sightseeing in one shopping cart
  • Thrillophilia — up to 30% on "experience + transport" bundles

2 · Direct local fleet or taxi unions

In the hills, you often must use the local taxi union — no aggregator app can overrule them — and you tie up directly with a rate contract.

  • Ladakh Taxi Union: government-published annual rate card; no B2B portal discount applies; 50% advance on long routes
  • Kashmir: fragmented by area — a Srinagar cab cannot legally do Gulmarg local sightseeing (that is a separate union's territory)
  • Spiti: no official government rate card; market-set by operators in Kaza and Manali
  • Tie-up = a simple rate-contract on ₹100 stamp paper (vehicle type, per-day or per-km rate, advance amount, cancellation terms)

3 · Self-drive apps — skip for hills

Zoomcar, Revv and MyChoize are city-only services with no coverage in Ladakh, Spiti or Uttarakhand mountain circuits, and they have no travel-agent program. Use only for a city pickup or drop-off leg, never for the hill route itself.

DMC fleets: most Ladakh, Spiti and Uttarakhand DMCs run their own 4–8 vehicles and will share a B2B rate sheet over WhatsApp — often the simplest and cheapest route of all.

Why the same Innova costs twice as much in Spiti as it does on the coast

A Toyota Innova Crysta runs roughly ₹3,000/day in Gokarna on the coast. The exact same vehicle costs ₹5,500–6,000/day in Spiti. The difference is not the vehicle — it is the terrain. Mountain routes mean severe wear on tyres and brakes, higher driver fatigue (narrow roads, altitude), longer driving hours for fewer kilometres covered, and fewer return-journey bookings for the driver (so they charge more per trip to cover dead mileage). Always cost your hill routes at mountain rates, not plains rates, or your package will run at a loss.

Per-trip booking vs seasonal rate-contract — which saves more

  • Per-trip: you pay per booking, make no volume commitment, but the unit cost is higher — good for testing a new route before you commit.
  • Rate-contract (seasonal or monthly): you lock in rates for the April–October hill season in exchange for a volume promise — typically 20–30% cheaper than walk-in per-trip rates.
  • Agent commission on transfers runs 10–30% (the lowest margin of any travel category); direct net-rate fleet tie-ups typically net you ~15–25%.

⊕ For Driftibo

For each destination, tie up directly with your DMC's fleet or the local union on a seasonal rate-contract — it is cheaper than aggregators and the only legally valid option in places like Ladakh. Keep TBO Transfers or Savaari only for city airport-to-hotel transfer legs. Price every hill route at its real day-rate (do not reuse a plains number), and always add the driver night-halt fee and any AC-per-km extras that the union rate cards charge — those are real costs that will bite you if you leave them out of your quote.
