Building & pricing a trip, and the documents you issue

This chapter shows you exactly how to turn a list of on-ground costs into a selling price that covers your margin and tax — worked all the way through with a real Chopta trip — and then tells you precisely which documents you must hand to travellers and collect from them before anyone boards a vehicle.

07

How to build & price a package — step by step

In plain words — what "costing a trip" actually means

Think of it like running a small catering business. You buy ingredients (the hotels, the car, the guide, the permits) at wholesale prices. You add a profit margin on top. Then the government adds its tax on top of that. The final number is what your customer pays. The only trick is knowing exactly which ingredients to count — because forgetting even one (like the forest entry slip at Chopta's trailhead) means that cost comes out of your pocket.

If you've never costed a trip: list every rupee you actually pay suppliers, mark it up to cover your profit, then add 5% GST on the total. That last number is the sticker price. Here it is worked all the way through.

How a trip deal actually moves from idea to invoice — a start-to-finish walk-through

Here is the journey of a real booking so you can picture every player and every rupee:

The life of a trip booking — in plain sentences

Step 1 — Someone sends you a WhatsApp. A couple in Delhi wants a long weekend in the mountains. They don't know exactly where; they just want something offbeat. This is your customer, and they have no idea what things cost on the ground.

Step 2 — You go to your supplier network. You have already called, WhatsApp-ed, or visited the people who actually run things on the ground: a homestay owner in Chopta who gives you a net rate (the wholesale price only you see), a local driver with a fixed charge for a Delhi–Chopta–Delhi run, a licensed trek guide who charges by the day, and a local contact who knows the forest permit fee. These are your ground handlers.

Step 3 — You build your cost sheet. You list every rupee you owe those suppliers (the net cost). You add a markup to cover your profit and your time. You add 5% GST on top of that final number. The total is what you quote.

Step 4 — You collect an advance and issue a GST invoice. The customer pays 30–50% upfront. You send them a proper tax invoice (the government requires this; see Section 08 below). The booking is now confirmed.

Step 5 — You pay your suppliers. You transfer the net cost amounts to the homestay, driver, and guide — usually closer to the travel date. The difference between what the customer paid you and what you pay suppliers is your gross profit.

Step 6 — You send a voucher pack. A few days before departure the customer gets: hotel confirmation details, the driver's phone number, the guide's contact, pickup time, what to pack, emergency contacts, and (for surprise trips) the destination reveal.

Step 7 — The trip runs. You are the coordinator if anything goes wrong. The balance payment is due before or on day of departure.

The money flow, simply: Customer pays you → You pay suppliers → What stays with you is your profit. Tax (GST) is collected on top of your price and remitted to the government; it is never your income.

The 8 cost components — what goes into your cost sheet

  1. Stay — per room per night (divide by 2 for two people sharing a twin room)
  2. Intercity transport — the car or tempo traveller plus driver's daily allowance (this single line is 40–50% of the total cost)
  3. Local / sightseeing transport — shared jeeps or autos for day trips within the destination
  4. Activities & entry fees — guide day-charge, monument entry fees
  5. Permits — ILP, forest fees (Chopta: ₹150/pax)
  6. Guide / trip leader — charged per day
  7. Meals — usually B+D, approximately ₹350 per person per meal
  8. Contingency — 5–8% buffer for Himalayan routes where weather, road closures, or permit delays can add unexpected costs

The formula — memorise this one line

Sticker price = Net Cost ÷ (1 − margin%) × 1.05

At a 20% gross margin that's a quick Net Cost × 1.3125 (a 25% markup, then ×1.05 for GST).

Markup ≠ margin (this trips up everyone): A 25% markup on your cost gives you only about a 20% margin on the price — because the base is different. The conversion formula: margin = markup ÷ (1 + markup). So: a 33% markup = 25% margin; a 43% markup = 30% margin.

Target ranges for offbeat India: group fixed-departures 15–20% margin · FIT/surprise trips 22–30% · luxury curated 35–40%. After you pay for your own time, marketing, and software, your net profit is roughly 8–15%.

FIT vs group — why the transport line changes everything

The biggest cost driver in any trip is intercity transport, because it is a fixed charge (the driver and fuel cost the same whether 2 or 10 people ride). So how many people share it completely changes your per-head cost:

  • Group departure (10 people, Tempo Traveller at ₹25,000): transport cost = ₹2,500 per person → total net cost per person ~₹9,500 → sell at ~₹12,500 per person
  • FIT booking (2 people, Innova at ₹16,000): transport cost = ₹8,000 per person → total net cost per person ~₹15,850 → sell at ~₹20,500 per person
  • FIT trips cost roughly 60% more per head than group trips. Most early WhatsApp enquiries you get will be FIT (couples, 2–3 friends). Do not quote group rates to small groups — you will lose money.
  • Group departures only become safe when you have a fill floor — at least about 8 of 10 seats filled — otherwise the fixed transport cost crushes your margin.

The surprise-trip pricing challenge — and how to solve it

For a surprise trip you cannot name a price for a specific destination the customer doesn't yet know. The solution is to price by tier band — a range of destinations grouped by rough cost:

  • Cost out the most expensive destination in each tier; that becomes your baseline price for the whole tier.
  • Apply your markup, then add 5% GST. If the destination you eventually reveal turns out to be cheaper, the gap becomes your buffer (5–8%) — it is never a loss.
  • Example tier bands: Tier A "Nearest Escape" (Chopta / Gokarna) ₹14,000–18,000 · Tier B "Mid-Distance" (Spiti / Ziro) ₹26,000–34,000 · Tier C "Far & Wild" (Ladakh / Meghalaya) ₹40,000–55,000 — all prices before GST.
  • Your invoice still describes the service fully, e.g. "4 nights twin accommodation, all transport, breakfast + dinner, 1 guide-day, permits — specific destination disclosed 7 days before departure." Your terms and conditions must clearly state that there is no refund after the destination is revealed.

The booking workflow — from first enquiry to voucher in the customer's hands

Every booking should travel through these five steps in this order. Skipping steps is where disputes and losses come from.

  • Day 0Enquiry arrives — reply within 2 hours. Capture the key facts: travel dates, number of people, rough budget band, any dietary restrictions or medical conditions. Use 5 WhatsApp questions or a Google Form — both work; pick one and stick to it.
  • Day 1Send an itinerary PDF. Day-by-day plan, clearly listing what is included and what is not. Mark it "Proposed — subject to confirmation" so nothing is legally binding yet.
  • Day 1–2Send the formal quote. Show the base price and the GST as two separate lines (not bundled). State the advance amount required, the balance due date, and that the price is held for 48 hours.
  • Day 2–3Confirm the booking and collect an advance. Take 30–50% of the total. Send a booking reference number and a proper GST tax invoice. The invoice should state that the price is inclusive of accommodation and transport.
  • 5–7 days before travelSend the full voucher pack. This is the set of documents the traveller carries. It includes: hotel booking confirmations, the driver's and guide's phone numbers, exact pickup time and location, a packing list for the terrain, emergency contacts for the destination, and — for surprise trips — the destination reveal at this point. Sending this well in advance cuts the volume of support questions you get by approximately 50–60%.

08

Documentation — what you issue & what you collect

In plain words — why the paperwork matters

Documents do three jobs at once. First, they protect you: a signed Terms & Conditions sheet is the difference between a customer who accepts a no-refund-after-reveal policy and one who disputes a bank charge. Second, they protect the traveller: a hotel voucher with a confirmation number means check-in happens smoothly even when you're unreachable at midnight. Third, they satisfy the government: the GST law requires specific fields on every invoice, and getting them wrong can trigger a notice years later. None of this paperwork is hard — it just needs to be done consistently.

Two stacks of paper run a clean agency: the documents you issue to the traveller, and the documents you collect from them. Get these right and a refund dispute, a checkpost, or a GST notice is a non-event.

What you ISSUE — and why each one matters (keep signed copies)

  1. Confirmed itinerary — the day-by-day plan with the hotel name and transport mode spelled out. This is the customer's reference if anything diverges on the ground.
  2. Quotation — the itemised price breakdown sent before the booking is confirmed. Keeping a copy of the quote protects you if a pricing dispute arises later.
  3. GST tax invoice — the legally required bill (see the GST Invoice Rule 46 fields box below). An invoice missing any of those fields is not valid: your client cannot use it for their own records, and it reflects poorly on your business. Without a compliant invoice, you cannot legally operate as a registered business and your customer cannot claim anything officially.
  4. Booking / hotel voucher — one voucher per supplier (hotel, transport, guide). The customer presents this to the supplier; it is the proof that you have already paid or guaranteed the booking.
  5. Terms & Conditions — the contractual rules: cancellation tiers, no-refund-after-reveal clause for surprise trips, exclusions. A WhatsApp message where the customer types "I agree" followed by your screenshot of the timestamp is legally acceptable in India.
  6. Receipt voucher — a mandatory document under GST Rule 50 for every advance payment you accept. Service advances attract GST the moment the money arrives, not on the day of travel.
  7. Permit printouts — any permits you have obtained on the group's behalf (forest entry, Inner Line Permit, Char Dham e-pass). Carry printed copies; many checkposts do not have reliable mobile data.
  8. TCS certificate (Form 27D) — for overseas trips only. You are not required to issue this for domestic India trips.

What you COLLECT from travellers — (take a copy, return their originals)

  • Government photo ID — Aadhaar is preferred plus one backup (Voter ID / Driving Licence / PAN / Passport). The BCAS list governs which IDs are accepted at airports; hotels and checkposts are generally more flexible.
  • Passport (for international trips) — must be valid for at least 6 months beyond the return date. Collect a photocopy of the bio-data page and the page with the most recent entry stamp.
  • Visa + travel insurance — where the destination country mandates either. Some destinations require proof of insurance at the port of entry.
  • Permits — each destination has its own:
    • ILP for Ziro (Arunachal Pradesh) and other restricted states
    • EDF receipt for Ladakh
    • Char Dham e-pass for Kedarnath / Badrinath / Gangotri / Yamunotri
    • For foreign nationals: PAP/RAP (Protected Area Permit / Restricted Area Permit)
  • Medical fitness certificate — required for Char Dham pilgrimage participants aged 50 and above, and strongly recommended (and sometimes required) for any trek above approximately 10,000 feet.
  • Minor NOC (No Objection Certificate) — required when a person under 18 is not travelling with both parents.

What a GST invoice must legally contain — GST Invoice Rule 46 ✓ Verified

Your invoice is a legal document. Missing any field below makes it invalid — your client cannot claim it, and it exposes you to a GST notice:

  • Your business name, address, and GSTIN (your GST registration number)
  • A unique invoice serial number (maximum 16 characters, restarted each financial year)
  • The invoice date
  • Recipient's name, address, and state code — if the recipient is unregistered (i.e., a normal customer, not a business) and the invoice is for ₹50,000 or more
  • SAC code (Services Accounting Code, 6 digits required if your annual turnover exceeds ₹5 crore; below that, 4 digits suffice): 998552 for tour packages · 998551 for ticket reservation · 998555 for visa/passport services
  • Description of the service sold
  • The taxable value (the price before GST)
  • CGST + SGST (if customer is in the same state) or IGST (if in a different state), with the applicable rate
  • Place of supply and the state code
  • Whether reverse charge applies (tick Yes or No — for most normal package sales the answer is No)
  • Your authorised signature
  • On the 5% GST slab, add the mandatory note: "ITC (Input Tax Credit) is not available to the recipient."
  • If your annual turnover exceeds ₹5 crore: you must generate an e-invoice through the government portal, which produces a QR code and an IRN (Invoice Reference Number)

Keep all invoice records for 72 months (6 years) — this is the legal requirement under CGST Section 36.

Per-trip document checklist ✓ Verified

Domestic trips (Chopta / Spiti / Gokarna / Ziro / Ladakh / Kashmir / Char Dham):

  • You issue: day-by-day itinerary · price quotation · GST tax invoice · hotel and transport vouchers · signed Terms & Conditions · receipt voucher for every advance payment received · permit printouts for the destination
  • You collect: photo ID (Aadhaar + one backup) · destination-specific permit IDs (ILP for Arunachal / EDF receipt for Ladakh / Char Dham e-pass) · medical fitness certificate for treks above 10,000 feet or Char Dham pilgrimage for age 50+ · travel insurance (strongly recommended even when not mandatory) · minor NOC if any traveller is under 18 without both parents

International trips — add these on top:

  • You also issue: a Form 27D TCS certificate within 15 days of the quarterly Form 143 (previously called Form 27EQ) due date. TCS rate is a flat 2% of the package value from 1 April 2026.
  • You also collect: passport (valid ≥6 months beyond return date) + visa + mandatory travel insurance for the destination + vaccination certificate if the destination country requires one
Char Dham e-pass — what changed for 2026

Registration for the four Char Dham shrines — Kedarnath, Badrinath, Gangotri, Yamunotri — plus Hemkund Sahib is mandatory but completely free at registrationandtouristcare.uk.gov.in. For the 2026 season the daily per-shrine pilgrim cap was removed by a Chief Minister order in April 2026 — so slot denial at registration is no longer a blocker this season. However, the portal opens approximately 6 weeks before the shrines open (it opened on 6 March in 2026), and peak May–June dates still fill within days, so register your group as early as possible. Use the "Tour Operator" mode on the portal to register each pilgrim individually (there is no bulk upload option). Commercial vehicles also require a separate Green Card from greencard.uk.gov.in. The e-pass is a QR code that is verified against the pilgrim's ID — it is not a fingerprint scan. The e-pass is a hard prerequisite: without it, the IRCTC (Indian Railway Catering and Tourism Corporation) helicopter booking system will not process your booking.

⊕ For Driftibo

For your domestic, Indian-traveller trips the document burden is genuinely light: a compliant GST invoice + a receipt voucher for each advance + a signed Terms & Conditions, plus the correct permit printouts for whichever destination you're operating. You have no TCS / Form 27D obligation at all until the day you sell an overseas package. Make the WhatsApp "I agree" message (confirming your T&C, with the cancellation tiers spelled out) a non-skippable step before you accept any advance payment — it is your single most powerful protection in a dispute.
