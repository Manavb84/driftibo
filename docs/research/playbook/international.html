Going international — selling outbound trips

This chapter shows you, step by step, how to start selling holidays abroad as a small Indian travel agency — where to source hotel rooms at wholesale prices without any airline licence, how to partner with local ground companies at each destination, what the tax and foreign-exchange rules actually mean in practice, and why all of this can run in parallel with your domestic business from day one.

15

Going international — inventory & tie-ups you can get now

Outbound isn't a "Year 2" thing. The honest truth flips the usual assumption: international inventory is easier to get than offbeat India, the trips are 2–4× higher-value, and the only thing that genuinely comes later is the tax and foreign-exchange paperwork — which triggers only when you sell an outbound trip, so it runs in parallel with everything else, not before it.

Why selling international holidays is easier than you think — in plain words

Think about the difference between finding a homestay in Chopta (a remote Uttarakhand village) versus finding a hotel in Dubai. The Chopta homestay is on no central system — you have to WhatsApp each property individually, there is no standard price, and there is no instant confirmation. A Dubai, Bali or Bangkok hotel, on the other hand, is listed on every major bedbank (wholesale hotel supplier) — you open a free account, search, click, and receive instant confirmation, often with no money owed upfront.

There is a second advantage: India is one of the world's biggest sources of outbound tourists — 33 million trips in 2025. International DMCs (Destination Management Companies) and national tourism boards actively seek out Indian travel agents. At a trade fair in Mumbai, their representatives will walk up to you.

And here is the key insight: getting access to inventory and dealing with tax compliance are two completely separate steps. You can open every bedbank and DMC account today, for free. The tax duties — TCS (Tax Collected at Source) and GST (Goods and Services Tax) — only start the day you actually take money for an outbound trip.

1 · The easy inventory — international bedbanks (day one, no airline licence needed)

In plain words: what a bedbank is and why it matters for your margins

A bedbank is the hotel equivalent of a cash-and-carry wholesale warehouse. Instead of buying one hotel room at the public price, the bedbank has already negotiated bulk deals with hundreds of thousands of hotels worldwide and offers you those rooms at a net rate (the trade price before your profit is added). You add your own markup on top and keep the difference as your margin. A typical bedbank net rate is 15–45% cheaper than the public price on a consumer booking site — and the gap widens the more upscale the hotel. The crucial point for a new agency: you do not need an IATA (International Air Transport Association) accreditation to access any of these bedbanks.

2 · The higher-margin route — partnering with a local ground company abroad (DMC tie-ups)

In plain words: what a DMC is and why the margins are better

A DMC (Destination Management Company) is like having a trusted, well-connected local partner at every destination you sell — someone who has already done all the hard work of contracting hotels, hiring drivers, arranging guides, and securing permits. They sell you the entire on-ground trip as one package at their trade price. You add your markup (typically 15–25%) and set the final customer price yourself.

Why are DMC margins better than booking via a bedbank alone? Because a DMC bundles everything together — hotel, transfers, guide, activities — and the higher combined package value means more rupees per booking. Outbound DMC packages typically yield 15–35% gross margin, compared with 8–15% for a domestic trip. And the total rupee amount per booking is 3–5× larger than a comparable domestic package.

How a DMC deal actually works — the full journey of a real booking

Here is a step-by-step walk-through so you can picture who does what, where the money moves, and who carries which risk:

  1. You find a DMC. At a trade fair like OTM Mumbai, through a directory like DMCFinder, or via a LinkedIn search. You send them your agency's GST registration certificate, your company's PAN (Permanent Account Number), and a link to your website. They review and typically approve you within 24–48 hours. No IATA accreditation is required.
  2. You receive a net-rate sheet. The DMC sends you a document listing their wholesale prices for different packages — a 4-night Bali trip, for example, including hotel, airport transfers, day tours, and a guide. This is their FIT (Fully Independent Travel) net rate. If you commit to a group of 10 or more and book 60–90 days in advance, you can ask for a lower GIT (Group Inclusive Tour) rate — typically 5–10% cheaper.
  3. You build your package and set your selling price. You take the DMC's net rate, add your markup (15–25% is typical), add international airfare sourced through a consolidator, and quote the customer a single all-inclusive price. The customer sees only your retail price — the DMC's net rate is your trade information.
  4. The customer pays you in rupees. You collect the full trip price. At this point the tax obligations begin: you collect TCS (currently 2% of the package from 1 April 2026), and your GST rate is 5% with no ITC (Input Tax Credit).
  5. You pay the DMC in foreign currency. You wire the money to the DMC abroad through your regular business bank account (any major Indian bank qualifies as an AD-I bank — Authorised Dealer Category-I bank) using a standard Form A2 remittance. No special forex licence is needed; no LRS (Liberalised Remittance Scheme) cap applies — provided you are operating as a registered company (Pvt Ltd or LLP, not as an individual sole proprietor). Incorporating cleanly avoids any ambiguity about LRS limits.
  6. The DMC delivers the on-ground experience. Hotels, transport, guides, and activities — all arranged and managed by the DMC. If a hotel is overbooked or a vehicle breaks down, the DMC carries responsibility for fixing ground-service failures. You carry the client-facing risk: making sure travellers were briefed correctly, that visas were arranged, and that flight disruptions are managed from your side.
  7. You issue a voucher. Once the DMC confirms the booking and accepts payment, they send you a voucher. You pass this to the traveller to carry on the trip.

Payment terms at the start: 100% prepaid (you pay the DMC before the trip) until you build a 3–6 month track record of clean payments. After that, you can negotiate 7–15-day credit. In the Maldives, the standard in the industry is a 25% deposit at booking, with the balance due 45 days before departure.

FINDING DMCs

Where to find and meet DMCs

  • Trade fairs (their reps come to you): OTM Mumbai (one of Asia's largest travel trade fairs — 2,200+ exhibitors, 60+ countries, free entry for trade visitors), SATTE Delhi (South Asia's Travel & Tourism Exchange), PATA Travel Mart (PATA — Pacific Asia Travel Association), ITB India (a branch of the international tourism trade fair), ATM Dubai (Arabian Travel Market), WTM London (World Travel Market), TTF (Travel & Tourism Fair, held across 8 Indian cities).
  • Open directories: DMCFinder — 10,000+ vetted DMCs across 120+ countries, free to search and contact directly.
  • Tourism-board approved-DMC lists: national tourism boards often publish lists of their approved operators — for example, the Azerbaijan tourism board, Georgia via Think Strawberries, Thailand via the TAT licence lookup; ETOA (European Tour Operators Association) for Europe.
  • Direct outreach: many South-East Asia DMCs accept a WhatsApp message with your proposed itinerary and respond with a net-rate sheet within 24 hours. A LinkedIn search for "DMC [destination] India B2B" surfaces representatives actively recruiting Indian agency partners.
DEAL STRUCTURE

What a DMC asks for and how the deal works

  • Documents they want: your GST certificate + company PAN + a website URL. That is usually all they need. Approval in 24–48 hours. No IATA accreditation required.
  • Pricing model: the DMC quotes you a net (FIT) rate; you add your own markup (15–25% is typical) and own the retail price. For groups of 10 or more booked 60–90 days ahead, ask for the lower GIT rate (5–10% less than FIT).
  • Payment terms: 100% prepaid by wallet or bank transfer when starting out; 7–15-day credit available after 3–6 months of clean payment history. Maldives norm: 25% deposit at booking, balance due 45 days before departure.
  • Who carries which risk: the DMC is responsible for on-ground service failures; you carry the client-facing risk (correct trip briefing, visa arrangements, managing flight disruptions).
  • Margins: outbound DMC packages yield 15–35% gross margin vs. 8–15% for a domestic trip — and the per-booking rupee value is 3–5× larger.

A note on trade body membership: the main Indian outbound trade body is OTOAI (Outbound Tour Operators Association of India), linked to UFTAA (Universal Federation of Travel Agents' Associations). Some DMCs abroad accept OTOAI membership as a substitute for IATA — but the earlier suggestion that this is easy to get was overstated: active OTOAI membership requires approximately ₹15 lakh in annual outbound sales, a physical office, and at least 1 year of trading history. A brand-new operator will not qualify on day one. You do not need it to start — GST certificate + PAN + a website is sufficient for most DMCs and every bedbank.

3 · Outbound flights & which destinations to start with

In plain words: booking international flights without an airline licence

You cannot issue an international airline ticket directly without an IATA accreditation. But you can book through a consolidator — a wholesale airline ticket seller who holds the IATA licence and issues the ticket on your behalf. Your customer gets a fully valid airline ticket. On international long-haul routes, consolidator discounts are substantial: roughly 8–18% off economy class, and 20–45% off business class compared with the public fare.

Long-haul air — using consolidators (no IATA needed)

  • Issue international tickets via the same B2B consolidators used for domestic flights: Riya Connect, TBO Air, Akbar Online, Mystifly. The consolidator's portal issues the ticket through the airline billing system; SOTO (Sold Outside / Ticketed Outside) fares — a pricing category for flights that originate outside India — mean consolidators can handle return and multi-leg journeys, not just outbound-from-India tickets.
  • The consolidator discount is deeper on long-haul routes: roughly 8–18% off economy class, 20–45% off business class.
  • A GST quirk in your favour: the international airfare itself is zero-rated for GST (the customer pays no GST on the actual ticket price). Your service fee on the air booking is taxed on a formula: 18% GST on a deemed 10% of the basic fare — which works out to roughly 1.8% effective GST on the total airfare value.

Best first outbound products (easy visa + deep bedbank coverage)

  • Malaysia — visa-free for 30 days (through December 2026), backed by a strong tourism-board push toward India.
  • Maldives — free VOA (Visa on Arrival — free, up to 90 days for Indians); essentially zero visa friction for the traveller.
  • Sri Lanka — free ETA (Electronic Travel Authorisation), just a 2–3 hour flight, and the cheapest entry-level international product available to Indians.
  • Vietnam — $25 e-visa, valid for 90 days; the fastest-growing outbound destination for Indians (+49%).

4 · What changes when you sell outbound (manageable — not a blocker)

In plain words: the three new compliance tasks that appear for outbound trips

When you sell an international package to an Indian customer, three new tasks appear that do not exist for domestic trips. First: you must collect a small extra tax (TCS) on top of the trip price and deposit it to the government. Second: your GST rate shifts to 5% with no offsetting credit. Third: you pay foreign suppliers in their currency using a standard bank form. None of these is complicated. Each is a well-defined, routine procedure. Think of them as three boxes to tick before your first outbound sale — not a reason to delay starting.

Tax & money rules you pick up

  • TCS — flat 2% from 1 April 2026 on overseas packages (reduced from the previous 5%/20% tier system; Income-tax Act 2025, Section 394(1)) ✓ Verified. Before your very first outbound sale, you must obtain a TAN (Tax Deduction and Collection Account Number) first — apply via Form 49B, cost ₹77, approximately 2–3 weeks processing; there is a ₹10,000 penalty for collecting TCS without one. Deposit the TCS to the government by the 7th of the following month, file Form 143 (formerly known as Form 27EQ), and issue the customer a TCS certificate so they can claim the credit in their annual income tax return.
  • GST on an outbound package sold to an Indian resident is 5% with no ITC (Input Tax Credit) — it is not zero-rated ✓ Verified.
  • Paying foreign DMCs and hotels is handled as a standard current-account business remittance through your normal AD-I bank (Authorised Dealer Category-I bank) using a Form A2 remittance and the correct purpose code — no special forex licence needed, and no LRS cap applies ✓ Verified. (Important caveat: incorporate as a company — if you operate as a sole proprietorship, outgoing payments could be counted against your personal LRS (Liberalised Remittance Scheme) limit of USD 250,000 per year. A Pvt Ltd or LLP company keeps all business remittances cleanly outside this cap.)
  • Want to also sell foreign currency directly to your customers? New FFMC (Full-Fledged Money Changer) licences are closed to new applicants as of April 2026. The alternative path: become a Forex Correspondent under an authorised bank and earn the exchange-rate spread without holding a separate licence.

Managing currency risk & the realistic start sequence

  • Currency rate risk: when a DMC invoices you in USD or EUR, any movement in the exchange rate between the day the customer pays you in rupees and the day you pay the DMC in foreign currency is your risk. You can hedge this by locking in today's rate using a bank forward contract, or you can price the foreign-currency leg at the exchange rate on the day of final payment — the common industry approach.
  • Day 1: open free accounts on Bedsonline + RateHawk + TBO Holidays; reach out to 1–2 DMCs in a visa-easy starter market (Malaysia, Maldives, or Sri Lanka).
  • Before your first outbound sale: apply for a TAN (Form 49B); confirm the 5% GST and 2% TCS treatment with your CA (Chartered Accountant).
  • Then sell: a single ₹90,000 Malaysia or Maldives surprise trip earns more gross margin than two ₹35,000 domestic bookings — this is the average-order-value fix that feeds the growth plan in §16.

Visa work as a service — helping your clients get their foreign visas

In plain words: what "visa facilitation" means and what your role actually is

When you offer a visa service to a client, your role is to prepare their application documents, fill in the required forms correctly, and submit everything to the relevant authority — then charge a fee for doing this legwork. You are not the embassy or consulate. The embassy makes the decision to grant or refuse the visa, and that decision is entirely outside your control. Your liability is limited to your service fee if something goes wrong administratively. There is no special government licence required to offer this facilitation service — you register on a partner portal or digital platform, set your own service fee, and add it on top of the official government and visa-centre charges.

One important nuance: charging a service fee to help an Indian citizen obtain a foreign country's visa is fully legitimate and widely practised. Charging to help a foreign national apply for an Indian e-Visa is not — the Indian government has not authorised private agents for this; do not offer it.

The 8-step workflow you run for every visa application

  1. Intake — confirm destination, visa type (tourist, business, multi-entry), travel timeline, and whether biometrics will be required in person
  2. Document checklist — passport valid for at least 6 months beyond the travel date, with at least 2 blank pages; photos to the exact specification; 3 months of bank statements; latest ITR (Income Tax Return) or recent salary slips; a letter from employer or from the business; confirmed flight bookings; hotel confirmation; travel insurance (Schengen applications require a minimum €30,000 of medical cover); a cover letter explaining the purpose and itinerary of the trip
  3. Form-filling — complete the embassy or VFS portal form; every data field must exactly match what is printed in the passport
  4. Fees — the consular fee (paid directly to the government); the VFS or BLS processing-centre fee; and your own service fee — charged separately and clearly
  5. Appointment — book the VAC slot (or VAYD or lounge appointment for premium clients)
  6. Biometrics — the client must attend in person to give fingerprints and have their photo taken; this step cannot be done by anyone else on their behalf
  7. Tracking — monitor application status through the portal; send the client WhatsApp updates at each key milestone so they are never left wondering
  8. Delivery — passport returned by courier or client collection from the VAC; keep a copy of all documents for your records

Biometrics must be done by your client in person. Only the traveller can give their fingerprints and have their photo taken — you cannot attend on their behalf, and no one else can substitute for them. Plan the appointment slot around your client's availability, not yours.

What to charge — and the disclaimers you must put in writing

Your own service fee — charged on top of all official government and processing-centre fees:

  • Easy e-visa or ETA (Bali, Vietnam, Sri Lanka): ₹500–1,500
  • Standard visa sticker (UAE, single Schengen country): ₹1,500–3,000
  • Interview-required visa (USA, UK, Canada, Australia): ₹3,000–5,000
  • Premium full-service (doorstep biometrics + courier + dedicated support throughout): ₹5,000–10,000; offer a 20–30% group discount for parties of 8 or more

Disclaimers — put these in writing before any money changes hands: you are facilitating the application; the embassy or consulate makes the sole approval decision; submitting the application is not a guarantee of approval; your liability is capped at your service fee if there is an administrative error; you are not responsible for courier loss of the passport; this service does not constitute legal advice or immigration advice.
