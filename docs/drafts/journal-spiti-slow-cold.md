# Spiti in October: What It's Like to Arrive at 4,200 Metres and Stop Thinking

**Kind:** Narrative dispatch  
**Suggested read time:** 7 min  
**Scene:** s-spiti

---

*The road from Shimla to Kaza takes eleven hours if the gods are kind and the boulders have stayed put. Most of that time you spend watching the landscape empty. Villages vanish. Trees thin to scrub. The river narrows. By the time you reach Nako, the sky is that shade of blue that only happens at altitude — a blue that looks like it was applied with a brush, with no atmosphere left to dilute it.*

*This is Spiti. And it is asking you, quietly but firmly, to slow down.*

---

## The First Thing You Notice

It's the quiet. Not the silence of a spa or a library — a silence that has depth to it, like the valley has been holding its breath since before anything alive was here. Spiti is a cold high-desert, a rain shadow behind the Great Himalayan Range, and the light arrives differently. Harder. Clearer. You don't squint because it's blinding — you squint because you're trying to take it in and there's too much of it.

The second thing you notice is the monastery. They're everywhere. Mud-brick, wind-stripped, painted in ochre and white, perched on cliffs that should not support anything. Key Gompa is the most photographed, but it's Dhankar — teetering above the confluence of the Spiti and Pin rivers — that stops most people cold. You stand below it and you can't quite believe it's not CGI.

---

## When to Go and What That Means

Spiti has two access windows. The summer route via Rohtang Pass (Manali) opens in June and closes by late October, sometimes earlier if the snow decides. The winter route via Shimla stays open year-round but is longer and harder. Most first-timers go in August or September, which is peak season — and peak season in Spiti means maybe twenty cars passing you in a day, not two hundred.

October is a different proposition. The tourists have thinned. The air is cold enough in the morning that your breath clouds the car window. But the sky has cleaned itself out after the monsoon, and on a clear day you can see the Kinnaur Kailash range from your guesthouse window, clear as a postcard, and it makes every morning feel unearned.

---

## What You'll Actually Do

The spine of a Spiti trip is the main road: Rekong Peo to Kaza, then north to Losar, east to Pin Valley if you have time. On this road, you stop at:

- **Nako Lake** — a glacial green lake at 3,662m, with a thousand-year-old monastery beside it. October mornings: fog on the water, no one around.
- **Dhankar Gompa** — perched, impossible, above the river confluence. The short trek up takes 25 minutes and earns you one of the best views in the Himalaya.
- **Key Monastery** — the big one. Active, living, students studying. Worth an hour and a conversation if you find a monk who wants to talk.
- **Kibber** — claimed for years as the highest motorable village in the world (it isn't, but it's up there). The meadow above it in October is empty and gold.
- **Pin Valley** — the other side of the range, accessed by a side road from Kaza. Colder, quieter, less visited. The snow leopard sightings happen in Pin in January and February, but even in October it's a different ecosystem entirely.

## What Spiti Does to You

This is the hard part to write, and the part that matters most.

Spiti has a way of returning you to a correct size. The mountains are so large and the sky is so persistent and the silence is so complete that within forty-eight hours you stop scrolling. Not because you've committed to a digital detox — there's no signal, so the decision is made for you. You stop scrolling, and then you start noticing things: the way the shadow of a cloud moves across a scree slope. The sound of a mule team on the road below. The specific quality of the light at 5:30am when the mountains go pink before anything else.

It's not dramatic. It doesn't feel like a revelation. It feels, if anything, like remembering something you knew before you got so busy.

---

## The Practical Bit

- **Best month:** October for clarity and emptiness. September for a touch more warmth.
- **Altitude acclimatisation:** Non-negotiable. The Shimla route is better for this — you gain altitude slowly. Fly into Bhuntar and drive to Kaza in a day only if you want a headache for 48 hours.
- **What to bring:** Warm layers you didn't think you'd need. A hat. Sunblock rated for altitude (the UV is brutal). Patience, for the roads.
- **What not to bring:** Expectations of itinerary-precision. Boulders happen. Gates close. The backup route adds three hours. This is not a problem. This is the trip.
